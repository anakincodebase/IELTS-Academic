# Band 3-5: Foundation Sentence Patterns (Weeks 1-2 Focus)

**Goal:** Master the 5 basic sentence patterns used in ALL English writing. These are your foundation.

---

## The 5 Essential Sentence Patterns

### Pattern 1: Subject + Verb (SV)

**Structure:** One person/thing does an action. That's it.

```
[Subject] + [Verb]

Examples:
- I sleep.
- Dogs bark.
- Birds fly.
- She walks.
- Rain falls.
```

**Why this matters:** This is the absolute foundation. Every complex sentence contains a simple SV structure.

**IELTS examples:**
- "Population increased." (describes a trend)
- "Unemployment fell." (describes a trend)
- "Prices rose." (describes a trend)

**Practice:** Write 5 SV sentences about your day.

---

### Pattern 2: Subject + Verb + Object (SVO)

**Structure:** One person/thing does an action TO something/someone.

```
[Subject] + [Verb] + [Object]

Examples:
- I eat bread.
- Dogs chase cats.
- She loves pizza.
- They study English.
- I write emails.
```

**Why this matters:** This is 80% of English sentences. Master this, and you're halfway to Band 9.

**IELTS examples (Task 1):**
- "The government implemented new policies."
- "Companies raised production levels."
- "Researchers conducted experiments."

**IELTS examples (Task 2):**
- "Society faces many challenges."
- "Technology changes lives."
- "Education provides opportunities."

**Practice:** Write 10 SVO sentences. Make sure each sentence has:
1. A clear subject (who/what?)
2. A clear verb (what action?)
3. A clear object (what receives the action?)

**Common errors to avoid:**
- ✗ "I eat." (incomplete for formal writing)
- ✓ "I eat breakfast." (complete SVO)
- ✗ "She study English." (wrong verb form)
- ✓ "She studies English." (correct verb agreement)

---

### Pattern 3: Subject + Linking Verb + Adjective/Noun (SLA)

**Structure:** One person/thing IS something (describes a quality or state).

```
[Subject] + [Linking Verb] + [Adjective or Noun]

Linking verbs: am, is, are, was, were, be, being, been
             appear, become, feel, grow, look, seem, smell, sound, taste, remain

Examples:
- I am happy.
- The weather is cold.
- She seems intelligent.
- They are engineers.
- The situation became complicated.
```

**Why this matters:** Descriptions, definitions, and state changes all use this pattern.

**IELTS examples (Task 1):**
- "The chart is comprehensive."
- "The data appears reliable."
- "The trend remains consistent."

**IELTS examples (Task 2):**
- "Education is essential."
- "Technology is transforming society."
- "The problem becomes more serious."

**Practice:** Write 8 SLA sentences using different linking verbs.

**Common errors:**
- ✗ "The graph show interesting." (wrong verb, wrong structure)
- ✓ "The graph is interesting." (correct SLA)
- ✗ "I am understand." (can't use linking verb here)
- ✓ "I understand." (correct SVO)

---

### Pattern 4: Subject + Verb + Indirect Object + Direct Object (SVIO/DO)

**Structure:** One person/thing gives/sends/shows something TO someone else.

```
[Subject] + [Verb] + [Indirect Object] + [Direct Object]

Examples:
- I gave him a book. (I = subject, gave = verb, him = indirect object, book = direct object)
- She told me the truth.
- They sent us information.
- The teacher gave us homework.
```

**Why this matters:** Used for communication and transfer of information.

**IELTS examples (less common, but appears):**
- "The government gave citizens new rights."
- "Researchers provided the team data."

**Practice:** Write 4 SVIO/DO sentences. Common verbs: give, tell, show, send, teach, buy, make, bring, write.

**Common errors:**
- ✗ "I gave a book him." (wrong word order)
- ✓ "I gave him a book." (correct SVIO/DO)

---

### Pattern 5: Subject + Verb + Object + Object Complement (SVOC)

**Structure:** One person/thing does an action, and that action changes or describes the object.

```
[Subject] + [Verb] + [Object] + [Complement]

Examples:
- I found the movie interesting. (the movie = object, interesting = what I think of it)
- They elected her president. (her = object, president = what she became)
- The company made him CEO. (him = object, CEO = his new role)
```

**Why this matters:** Describes opinions, perceptions, and role changes.

**IELTS examples:**
- "Research has proven this method effective."
- "The study found the results significant."

**Practice:** Write 4 SVOC sentences. Common verbs: find, make, consider, think, judge, believe, elect, call, name.

**Common errors:**
- ✗ "I found interesting the movie." (wrong word order)
- ✓ "I found the movie interesting." (correct SVOC)

## Week 1 Drilling: Build Automaticity

**Goal:** Make these 5 patterns automatic. When you write, you don't think—you just use the right pattern.

### Daily Drill (20 minutes)

**Monday-Tuesday: SV & SVO**
- Write 5 SV sentences (basic facts)
- Write 10 SVO sentences (actions with objects)
- Check: Does each sentence have clear subject, verb, object?

**Wednesday-Thursday: SLA**
- Write 5 sentences with "is/are"
- Write 5 sentences with other linking verbs (become, seem, appear, feel)
- Check: Is the adjective/noun a description of the subject?

**Friday: SVIO/DO & SVOC**
- Write 4 SVIO/DO sentences (give, tell, show, send)
- Write 4 SVOC sentences (find, make, consider)

**Saturday-Sunday: Mixed Review**
- Write 15 sentences using ANY of the 5 patterns
- You choose which pattern fits the meaning

### Weekly Goal: 60 Correct Sentences

By end of Week 1, you should have written 60+ sentences using these patterns with NO errors. This seems like a lot, but it's how automaticity is built.

## Word Order Matters

English word order is STRICT. Sentences follow predictable patterns.

**Example: Why Order Matters**

"I gave him a book" = normal, clear
"I gave a book him" = wrong, sounds broken
"A book him I gave" = very wrong, sounds ancient or poetic

**The three positions that matter:**
1. **Subject position (before verb):** Actor, doer
2. **Verb position (middle):** The action
3. **Object position (after verb):** Receiver of action

Always: **Subject → Verb → Object**

## Common Band 3-4 Mistakes (Fix These NOW)

### Mistake 1: Incomplete Sentences (Missing Object)

**✗ Band 3:** "The government implemented."  
**✓ Band 5:** "The government implemented new policies."

Missing object = sentence feels unfinished.

### Mistake 2: Wrong Verb Form

**✗ Band 3:** "She go to school."  
**✓ Band 5:** "She goes to school."

Subject-verb agreement: Add "s" to verb when subject is 3rd person singular (he, she, it).

### Mistake 3: Mixing Patterns Without Connecting Words

**✗ Band 3:** "I study English I love languages."  
**✓ Band 5:** "I study English because I love languages."

Two complete sentences need a conjunction (and, but, because, so) or punctuation (semicolon, period).

### Mistake 4: Using the Wrong Linking Verb

**✗ Band 3:** "The data shows reliable."  
**✓ Band 5:** "The data appears reliable." OR "The data is reliable."

"Shows" is not a linking verb. Use "is," "appears," "seems," "looks."

### Mistake 5: Adjective-Adverb Confusion

**✗ Band 3:** "She speaks English very good."  
**✓ Band 5:** "She speaks English very well."

After an action verb, use an adverb (well, quickly, clearly). After a linking verb, use an adjective (good, quick, clear).

## Key Takeaway

**These 5 patterns are in EVERY English sentence.**

Complex sentences are just these patterns combined with connecting words (and, but, because, when, if, while).

Master these 5, and complex sentences become easy.

Start today. Write 15 sentences using these patterns. Check for accuracy. Do this every day for one week.

By week 2, simple sentence formation will feel automatic. Then you'll be ready to combine sentences into complex structures.
