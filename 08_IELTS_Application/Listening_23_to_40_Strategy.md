# Listening: From 23/40 to 40/40 (Band 6.5 → Band 9)

You're at **23/40 (Band 6.5)**. Your listening foundation exists, but three critical gaps prevent you from reaching 40/40.

This guide identifies exactly what separates your current level from Band 9, then provides the drills to close each gap in 2-3 weeks.

---

## Why You're Stuck at 23/40: The Three Barriers

### Barrier 1: Missing Words Despite Knowing Them (40% of your loss)

You know the word "accommodation" but miss it in the recording because:
- You've only studied the spelling, not the sound at normal speed
- You expected /æk-ə-mə-ˈdeɪ-ʃən/ but heard /ə-kɒm-əˈdeɪʃən/ (faster, weaker vowels)
- By the time you realize what you heard, the next word is already playing

**Solution:** Pre-listen word drills (below) that force your brain to recognize words at EXAM SPEED before you take practice tests.

### Barrier 2: Not Reading the Questions Strategically (30% of your loss)

You read questions WHILE listening. This splits your attention. By the time you finish reading question 5, you've missed the answer to question 4.

**Solution:** Pre-read strategically (detailed drill below).

### Barrier 3: Weak Spelling Confidence (20% of your loss)

You hear the right word but spell it wrong (or leave it blank from doubt). "Accommodate" vs. "Acommodate"—you're not sure and panic.

**Solution:** Intensive spelling drilling (detailed below).

### Barrier 4: Not Distinguishing Between Sections (10% of your loss)

Sections 1-2 are easier but less important (lower scoring weight). Sections 3-4 are where 40/40 is won or lost. You're spending equal energy on all sections.

**Solution:** Weighted practice (detailed below).

## DRILL 1: Pre-Listening Word Recognition (Do This DAILY for 1 week)

**Time:** 10 minutes/day  
**Goal:** Train your brain to recognize words at exam speed BEFORE taking a practice test

### How It Works

1. Choose 5 high-frequency IELTS words (see list below)
2. Hear the word at SLOW speed (0.75x)
3. Hear it at NORMAL speed (1.0x)
4. Hear it at FAST speed (1.25x)
5. Write the word

**Why this works:** By the time you take a practice test, your brain has pre-patterns recognition. You'll catch words you previously missed.

- Listen 3 times at each speed
- Write the word without looking
- Repeat for all 5 words
- Total: 10 minutes

## DRILL 2: Strategic Pre-Reading (Do This BEFORE Every Practice Test)

**Time:** 3-4 minutes  
**Goal:** Know what to listen for BEFORE the recording starts

### The Pre-Reading System

**Step 1: Skim All Questions (Don't Read Carefully Yet)**
- Sections 1-2: You have 30 seconds before the recording. Scan question types (Fill-a-form? Multiple choice? Matching?)
- Section 3-4: Skim question stems. Look for repeated words (these are clues to when answers are coming).

**Step 2: Identify the Answer Type**
- Form filling? Listen for: names, addresses, phone numbers, email, dates, prices
- Multiple choice? Listen for: option keywords from A/B/C
- Matching? Listen for: keywords from the list you're matching

**Step 3: Mark "Difficult" Questions**
- If a question uses unfamiliar vocabulary, mark it. That's your high-risk question.
- Example: "Which certification does the candidate NOT hold?" → Hard question. Needs careful listening.

**Step 4: Time Management**
- Section 1 (easy): Skim questions 1-10 quickly. Allocate 2 minutes max to pre-read. Save your mental energy for Section 3-4.
- Section 3 (hard): Pre-read questions carefully. Allocate 3 minutes. Sections 3-4 determine your score.

## DRILL 4: Weighted Section Practice (Do This 2x per week)

**Why:** Sections 3 and 4 determine your final score. At 23/40, you're probably scoring:
- Section 1: 8/8 or 7/8 (easy)
- Section 2: 6/8 (moderate)
- Section 3: 5/8 (hard—THIS IS YOUR PROBLEM)
- Section 4: 4/8 (very hard—THIS IS YOUR PROBLEM)

### The Weighted Practice Plan

**Monday (Full test, but track by section):**
- Take a FULL practice test
- Score each section separately
- Identify: Which section has the most errors?

**If Section 3 is weak (5/8 or below):**
- Use NEXT session to do Section 3 practice ONLY (repeat 3-4 different Section 3s back-to-back)
- Focus on: Academic discussion, multiple speakers, changing topics

**If Section 4 is weak (4/8 or below):**
- Use NEXT session to do Section 4 practice ONLY (repeat 3-4 different Section 4s back-to-back)
- Focus on: Lectures, following a single speaker for 8 minutes, note-taking pace

**Critical insight:** You can't improve Section 3 by doing more Section 1. You improve Section 3 by intensively practicing Section 3.

## DRILL 5: Connected Speech Pattern Drills (Do This 3x per week)

**Time:** 5 minutes  
**Goal:** Recognize words when they're linked, weakened, or elided

### Pattern 1: Weak Forms (The "heard but didn't recognize" trap)

**Listen for these transformations:**

"**to**" becomes /tə/ not /tuː/:
- "I want **to** go" → sounds like "want-uh-go"

"**have**" becomes /əv/ or disappears:
- "I should **have** gone" → sounds like "should-uh-gone"

"**was**" becomes /əz/:
- "She **was** happy" → sounds like "shuh-happy"

**Drill:** YouTube search "English weak forms" + download 5 minutes of examples. Listen 3x daily for 3 days.

### Pattern 2: Linking (Consonant + Vowel blend)

**Listen for these:**
- "pick **i**t" → sounds like "pick-it" (but LINKED, not separate)
- "let **u**s" → sounds like "lettus" (one unit)

**Drill:** Take one official Section 3 (academic conversation). Listen at 0.75x speed. Every time you hear linking, pause and repeat. Do this for 1 section, 2x per week.

### Pattern 3: Elision (Sounds Drop Out)

**Listen for these:**
- "nex**t** day" → sounds like "nex-day" (the /t/ disappears)
- "bes**t** friend" → sounds like "bes-friend"

**Drill:** Same as linking drill. Focus on "did-uh-go" (not "did-you-go"), "fact-sheet" (not "fact-sheet").

## Section-by-Section Tactical Guide

### Section 1 (Easy - Form Filling)
**Goal:** 8/8

**What they test:**
- Names, addresses, phone numbers, email, dates, prices
- Listening to someone spell things slowly

**Your strategy:**
- During pre-reading: anticipate what information is coming. "Question 3 asks for a phone number" → be ready to hear digits
- During listening: write EVERYTHING. Even if it seems obvious, write it. "They said their name is John"—write "John"
- Common mistake at 23/40: Missing digits in phone numbers (e.g., "555 0-1-2" and you write "551012" instead of "5550122")

**Improvement focus:** Section 1 is already strong for you probably (7-8/8). Don't waste time here. Move to Section 3.

### Section 2 (Moderate - Monologue)
**Goal:** 8/8

**What they test:**
- Single speaker describing a place, event, or service
- Map labeling, short MCQs, note-taking

**Your strategy:**
- Pre-read the questions and anticipate: "Is this about a building layout?" → listen for direction words (left, right, near, opposite)
- Common mistake: Distracted by accent. At 23/40, you're probably missing words because you're focusing on the accent instead of the meaning.
- **Fix:** Listen to the CONTENT not the accent. Practice with non-native speakers (YouTube, podcasts).

**Improvement focus:** If you're at 6/8 here, aim for 7-8/8 with weighted Section 2 practice (2x per week).

### Section 3 (Hard - Academic Discussion)
**Goal:** 8/8

**What they test:**
- 2-4 speakers discussing a project, research, or academic topic
- MCQs, matching, short-answer questions
- Speakers change topics, interrupt each other, disagree

**Your strategy:**
1. **Pre-read ruthlessly:** Before the recording, read ALL questions. Mark keywords that signal answers.
2. **Identify the speakers:** First 10 seconds, speakers introduce themselves. Write their names/roles.
3. **Track topic changes:** Section 3 has 4-5 topic shifts. When you hear "So moving on to..." or "Let's talk about..." → new topic, new questions coming.
4. **Don't chase clarity:** If you miss a word, MOVE ON. Dwelling on it causes you to miss the next answer.
5. **Common mistake at 23/40:** You're not taking notes fast enough. Multiple speakers talk fast. You're writing word-for-word instead of keywords.
   - Band 6 note: "The project will involve investigating the effects of climate change on agriculture..."
   - Band 9 note: "Project = climate + agriculture"
   - Your notes are too long. Speed is the problem, not comprehension.

**Improvement focus:** This is your HIGHEST PRIORITY. Spend 60% of your practice time on Section 3 (repeated drills, weighted practice).

### Section 4 (Very Hard - University Lecture)
**Goal:** 8/8

**What they test:**
- Single speaker delivering a 7-8 minute lecture on an academic subject
- Sentence completion, summary completion, note-taking
- No breaks. You must track questions 31-40 continuously while listening

**Your strategy:**
1. **Pre-read questions 31-40 carefully:** This is your map. The questions are in order—answer 31 happens first in the recording, answer 40 happens last.
2. **Listen for staging language:** "Firstly," "Next," "Finally," "In conclusion." These signal where you are in the questions.
3. **Note-taking speed is CRITICAL:** You have ~45 seconds per question to hear the answer. Faster notes = faster thinking.
4. **Common mistake at 23/40:** You're trying to understand EVERYTHING the lecturer says. But the exam only tests 10 points. Ignore 80% of the content. Listen for the 10 answers only.

**Improvement focus:** Section 4 is your second priority. Spend 25% of practice time here. Do 3-4 Section 4s back-to-back (same day) to build stamina.

## Exam-Day Protocol

**30 minutes before Section 1:**
- Scan all questions in the booklet once (don't read deeply)
- Do the pre-listening word recognition warm-up (5 words, 2 minutes)
- Mentally note: "Section 1 = form; Sections 2 = map; Sections 3 = discussion; Section 4 = lecture"

**During the exam:**
- Write EVERYTHING. Erase later if needed. Blank answers = 0 points.
- If you miss one answer, MOVE ON immediately. Don't dwell.
- After each section (during the pause), glance at next section's questions briefly. Then relax for 20 seconds.

**During Section 4:**
- This is the hardest and longest. You'll be tired. Push through. Section 4 questions are slightly weighted.
- Don't panic if it feels blurry. Everyone feels that. Band 9 scorers are just slightly less panicked.
