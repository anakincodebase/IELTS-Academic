# <span style="color: #1a3a52">IELTS Writing Task 2 Master Guide</span>

Task 2 is not testing memorization. It's testing whether you can think clearly and communicate that thinking within strict time limits. You have 250 words minimum and 40 minutes to deliver a clear argument.

The golden rule is simple: answer the question directly. Stay consistent throughout. That's it.

---

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Strategy: The 5-Step Acceleration System</span>

Most test-takers waste time studying everything instead of fixing what's actually holding them back. If you already score Band 7 on grammar, studying basic subject-verb agreement is wasted effort.

1. **Clarify your target.** What score do you need?
2. **Eliminate wasted study.** If you're already strong in cohesion, focus elsewhere.
3. **Find your exact weakness.** Is it Task Response? Vocabulary range? Grammar accuracy? Identify the specific criterion holding you back.
4. **Fix the weakness before practicing more essays.** Doing 10 full timed essays without fixing root errors teaches bad habits.
5. **Return to timed practice once the weakness improves.** Now the practice trains you properly.

Repeating full essays without analyzing what actually failed is how students stay stuck at Band 6.

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Scoring Reality: The Four Criteria</span>

Your score on Task 2 comes from four separate assessments. You need all four to be near your target band.

**Task Response:** Did you actually answer the question? Did you take a position? Did you stay consistent?

**Coherence and Cohesion:** Do ideas flow logically? Can the reader follow your argument?

**Lexical Resource:** Do you use sophisticated vocabulary accurately? Or do you force complex words and get them wrong?

**Grammatical Range and Accuracy:** Do you use diverse grammar structures without errors? Or are there so many small mistakes that accuracy tanks?

Most students don't have a "general English" problem. They have one or two specific weaknesses in these four criteria. Your job is to find yours.

## <span style="color: #d4af37">The Biggest Myths That Lower Scores</span>

### Myth 1: Use many C1/C2 words to get high band
Reality:
- Natural and accurate vocabulary beats forced advanced words.
- Wrong collocation, spelling, or meaning drops score.

### Myth 2: Use many linking words everywhere
Reality:
- Over-linking sounds mechanical.
- Use connectors only when logic needs them.

### Myth 3: Range is more important than accuracy
Reality:
- Both matter.
- Frequent error-free sentences are essential for 7+.

### Myth 4: Never repeat words
Reality:
- Controlled repetition is normal and often necessary.
- Replace only when you are fully sure of synonym accuracy.

### Myth 5: Use hooks and memorized templates in introduction
Reality:
- "Hotly debated" style hooks waste time and sound memorized.
- Memorized language can be penalized.

### Myth 6: Add fake statistics to look academic
Reality:
- Weak or fake data harms credibility.
- Realistic, clear examples are safer and stronger.

### Myth 7: Use idioms in Task 2 writing
Reality:
- Idioms are mostly for speaking or informal contexts.
- Task 2 is formal argument writing.

### Myth 8: More words always means higher score
Reality:
- Minimum is 250 words.
- Very long essays often lose focus and increase error count.

## <span style="color: #00838f">The 5 IELTS Essay Types (and Exact Logic)</span>

### Agree or Disagree
- For extent questions (To what extent do you agree?), choose a clear side.
- Strong strategy: choose the position you can defend with clearer ideas and evidence.
- A partial position is valid if your thesis and paragraph logic stay consistent.

### Advantage and Disadvantage
- Direct form (positive or negative development): cover both advantages and disadvantages in balanced depth.
- Outweigh form (Do advantages outweigh disadvantages?): choose a winning side and give more development to the winning side.

### Discuss Both Views and Give Your Opinion
- Body paragraph 1: explain View A.
- Body paragraph 2: explain View B.
- Conclusion: state your personal opinion clearly.

### Problem and Solution
- If the prompt asks only for solutions, focus only on solutions.
- If it asks causes and solutions, use one paragraph for causes and one for solutions.

### Direct / Two-Part Question
- Answer both questions directly and explicitly.
- If one question is ignored, Task Response drops sharply.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">POER Time Management Method (6 to 7 Minutes)</span>

### P: Planning
- Read the statement and question carefully.
- Underline key words.
- Trigger lexical resource by thinking of synonyms immediately.

### O: Organization
- Decide your side/position.
- Select one to two core concepts for body paragraphs.

### E: Evaluation
- After writing, check logical flow from introduction to conclusion.
- Confirm every paragraph supports the thesis.

### R: Revision
- Check grammar and spelling.
- High-value trick: read backward (conclusion -> body -> introduction) to catch surface errors.

### Introduction (3 sentences, advanced paraphrasing)
Do not write a one-line paraphrase only.

1. Sentence 1: generic factual setup for concept one.
2. Sentence 2: introduce concept two (for example, Many people believe that...).
3. Sentence 3 (thesis): clear opinion and essay direction.

### Body Paragraphs (one paragraph = one concept)
Each body paragraph must contain exactly three elements:

1. Topic sentence (paragraph heading idea)
2. Explanation (logical development)
3. Example (personal, others, or trend-based)

> [!info] Rule
> One well-developed concept is stronger than multiple shallow points.

### Conclusion (2 sentences)
1. Restate position with a clear concluding phrase.
2. Summarize the key arguments briefly.

Do not introduce new ideas in conclusion.

Special note for Discuss Both Views essays:
- You may add a brief QRM-style ending (Question + Real-world scenario + Message) as a polished close.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Idea Generation Without Panic</span>
### What is a good idea?
A good idea must be:
- relevant to question
- easy to explain
- quick to think of under exam timing

### Avoid unproductive brainstorming
- Random idea listing creates irrelevant points.
- Use focused thinking instead.

### Method A: Coffee Shop Method
Imagine explaining the question to a friend in a cafe.
- Use normal, simple logic.
- Pick obvious, defensible ideas.

### Method B: Family Fortunes Method
Ask yourself:
- If 100 people answered this, what are top 1-2 common answers?

> [!info] Rule
> In IELTS, simple and logical often scores better than complex and unclear.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">How to Build Strong Examples</span>
### Example generation system
1. Think of a real example from life/news/work.
2. Check if it directly supports your main point.
3. If too personal, widen the sample.

Transform pattern:
- Too personal: "My brother..."
- Better: "Many people in my city/country/profession..."

### Example quality checklist
- Is it believable?
- Is it linked to the paragraph idea?
- Does it help the examiner understand quickly?

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Type-Specific Execution Recap</span>
## Opinion (Agree/Disagree)
- Declare one clear position in introduction.
- Keep stance consistent across both body paragraphs and conclusion.

## Advantage/Disadvantage
- Direct AD: balanced treatment of both sides.
- Outweigh AD: stronger development for your winning side.

## Discuss Both Views + Opinion
- Paragraph 1: View A.
- Paragraph 2: View B.
- Conclusion: your explicit personal stance.

## Problem/Solution
- Only solutions if prompt asks only solutions.
- Separate causes and solutions when both are asked.

## Direct Two-Part Question
- Answer both parts directly with clear paragraph mapping.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Accuracy Before Speed</span>
### Improve in this sequence
1. Accuracy and clarity
2. Development quality
3. Timing control

### Timed acceleration model
- Start untimed while fixing weak areas.
- Then reduce duration gradually (for example 60 -> 55 -> 50 -> 45 -> 40 mins).
- Book test only when target score is stable under timing.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Sources and Practice Quality Control</span>
Use official sources only:
- Cambridge IELTS books
- IDP official materials
- British Council official materials
- IELTS.org resources

> [!warning] Quality Control
> Many unofficial online tests and sample essays are misleading.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Final 5-Minute Self-Check (Task 2)</span>
- Did I answer every part of the question?
- Is my position clear and consistent?
- Does each body paragraph have one main developed idea?
- Are examples relevant and believable?
- Did I overuse linking words?
- Did I avoid forced advanced vocabulary?
- Did I remove obvious grammar and spelling errors?
- Did I follow POER (especially revision backward read)?

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Band 8.5 to 9 Operating Checklist</span>
- I use clear paraphrasing, not memorized templates.
- I prioritize accuracy over decorative language.
- I use natural vocabulary and precise collocations.
- I keep one central idea per body paragraph.
- I can generate relevant ideas quickly with focused methods.
- I can produce full essays in exam time without losing clarity.
