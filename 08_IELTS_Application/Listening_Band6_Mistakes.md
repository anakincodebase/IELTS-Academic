# <span style="color: #1a3a52">IELTS Listening: Why You Miss Answers (Band 6 Mistakes)</span>

At 23/40, you're making specific, predictable errors. This file identifies all of them.

---

## <span style="color: #d4af37">The 10 Biggest Mistakes at Band 6 Level</span>

### <span style="color: #d4af37">Mistake 1: Writing Down Words That Sound Similar (Wrong Spelling = 0 Points)</span>

**What happens:** You hear "accommodation" and write "accomodation" (missing one 'c'). The examiner marks it wrong. You get 0 points.

**Why:** You know the word, but under exam pressure, you spell it phonetically instead of correctly.

**Fix:**
- Do the spelling drills in [Listening_Spelling_Bank_200_Words.md](Listening_Spelling_Bank_200_Words.md)
- Before exam: Review the 80 academic words (accommodation, achievement, etc.) 3x
- During exam: Write confidently. If you're unsure of spelling, write your best guess (something is better than blank)

**Examples of spelling that lost you marks:**
- "accommodation" → you wrote "acommodation"
- "commitment" → you wrote "comitment"
- "necessary" → you wrote "neccessary"
- "separate" → you wrote "seperate"
- "maintenance" → you wrote "maintainance"

---

### <span style="color: #d4af37">Mistake 2: Frozen When You Hear British Accents (Especially Section 2)</span>

**What happens:** The speaker has a strong Scottish or Irish accent. You focus on "decoding" the accent instead of listening for meaning. You miss the next 3 answers.

**Why:** You're treating the accent as the problem, not your weak accent exposure.

**Fix:**
- Watch British YouTube channels (UK university lectures, BBC nature documentaries)
- Listen to TED talks by British speakers (1.0x speed)
- Accept that accents change word sounds. "Th" becomes "V" in some accents. "R" disappears. Learn to ignore these shifts and listen for meaning.

**Example accent differences:**
- American: "To-MAH-to" | British: "tuh-MAR-tuh"
- American: "HERB" | British: "ERB"
- Your ear needs time to adjust. Give it 10 hours of British accent listening.

### <span style="color: #d4af37">Mistake 3: Missing Words Because You Didn't Pre-Read Questions</span>

**What happens:**
- Question 5: "What time does the accommodation become available?"
- You're still thinking about Question 4's answer when they mention the time for Question 5.
- You miss it.

**Why:** You're reading questions WHILE listening instead of BEFORE listening.

**Fix:**
- Sections 1-2: You have 30 seconds before recording. Read ALL questions in those sections.
- Section 3: Read all questions in Section 3 before they start talking.
- Section 4: Read questions 31-40 before the lecture begins.

**Time breakdown:**
- Section 1: 30 seconds pre-read. You'll know: form-filling, phone numbers, names
- Section 2: 30 seconds pre-read. You'll know: map/location, single speaker
- Section 3: 45 seconds pre-read (after Section 2 ends). You'll know: multiple speakers, topic
- Section 4: 30 seconds pre-read (after Section 3 ends). You'll know: lecture, single speaker

**Result:** Your brain is primed. When the answer comes, you're listening for it, not discovering it.

### <span style="color: #d4af37">Mistake 4: Writing Too Much During Listening</span>

**What happens:**
- Speaker says: "The project will investigate the effects of climate change on agricultural productivity in developing nations."
- You write: "The project will investigate the effects of climate change on agricultural productivity in developing nations."
- You're still writing when they've moved to the next question.
- You miss it.

**Why:** You're note-taking word-for-word instead of capturing key information.

**Fix:**
- Write KEYWORDS only:
  - "The project will investigate..." → Write: "Project = climate + agriculture"
  - "We need to consider..." → Write: "consider = ?"
  - "The main issue is..." → Write: "MAIN ISSUE"

- Use abbreviations:
  - "government" → "govt"
  - "communication" → "comm"
  - "significant" → "sig"
  - "approximately" → "~"
  - "because" → "bc"

**Example conversion:**

**Band 6 note-taking:**
"The committee decided to increase funding for research projects in renewable energy to meet international environmental standards."

**Band 8 note-taking:**
"Committee → ↑ funding, renewable energy, intl standards"

**Practice:** Take a Section 3 recording. Listen at 0.75x speed. Only write keywords. You'll naturally speed up.

### <span style="color: #d4af37">Mistake 5: Missing Synonyms (Exam Uses Different Word Than Question Shows)</span>

**What happens:**
- Question says: "What is the student's main concern?"
- Speaker says: "The biggest issue for me is..." (NOT "concern," but same meaning)
- You think: "That's not the right word, so it's not the answer"
- You miss it.

**Why:** You're looking for exact word matches instead of understanding meaning.

**Fix:**
- During pre-reading, mark "opposite words" that might appear:
  - "concern" ← → "issue," "problem," "worry," "difficulty"
  - "benefit" ← → "advantage," "positive," "good thing"
  - "increase" ← → "rise," "go up," "grow," "expand"
  - "expensive" ← → "costly," "high-priced," "dear"

- Train yourself: When you hear a synonym, mentally note it.

**Example pairs (study these):**

| Question Word | Speaker Might Say |
|---|---|
| location | place, site, area, position |
| cost | price, expense, fee, charge |
| improve | better, enhance, develop, upgrade |
| difficult | hard, challenging, tough, tricky |
| advantage | benefit, positive, good point, strength |
| disadvantage | drawback, problem, weakness, downside |
| important | significant, crucial, key, essential |
| interesting | fascinating, compelling, engaging, captivating |
| busy | crowded, congested, full, packed |
| quiet | peaceful, calm, silent, tranquil |

**Practice:** Take one Section 3 or 4. List all question keywords. Before listening, write possible synonyms. Then listen and check: Did they use a synonym?

### Mistake 6: Confusing Similar-Sounding Words

**What happens:**
- Question asks for "a stationary object" (not moving)
- Speaker says "stationery supplies" (paper/pens)
- You write: "stationery"
- Examiner marks it wrong.

**Why:** Under pressure, your ear doesn't distinguish between homophones.

**Fix:**
- Study homophone pairs (there/their/they're, to/too/two, etc.)
- In context, most homophones are obvious. "Stationery supplies" = STATIONERY (supplies). You won't accidentally write that if you understand meaning.
- Context is your safety net.

**Common homophones that confuse Band 6:**

| Pair | Pronunciation | Meaning | IELTS Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| stationary / stationery | /ˈsteɪ-ʃən-rer-i/ | not moving / paper/pens | "Stationary object" / "Buy stationery supplies" |
| principle / principal | /ˈprɪn-sə-pəl/ | rule / chief | "The principle is..." / "School principal" |
| council / counsel | /ˈkaʊn-səl/ | group / advice | "City council" / "Seek counsel" |
| allowed / aloud | /ə-ˈlaʊd/ | permitted / out loud | "Not allowed" / "Read aloud" |
| accept / except | /ə-ˈsept/ | receive / exclude | "Accept the offer" / "Everyone except John" |

### Mistake 7: Missing Sentences That Start With "However," "But," "Although"

**What happens:**
- Speaker: "The hotel has excellent facilities. However, it's quite expensive."
- You hear: "excellent facilities" → you mark that as the answer
- You miss: "However, it's expensive" → the real point

**Why:** You're capturing what you hear first, missing the correction/contrast that follows.

**Fix:**
- Listen for "however," "but," "although," "yet," "instead," "in contrast"
- These ALWAYS signal: "The previous thing was wrong or incomplete. Listen for what comes next."
- During pre-reading: If a question asks "What is the main disadvantage?" and the speaker says "It has benefits. However, the main disadvantage is...", you know the disadvantage is coming AFTER "however."

**Training:**
- YouTube search: "English contrast words" (however, but, although, etc.)
- Listen for the pattern: Positive statement → "However" → Negative statement
- The second part is usually the answer.

### Mistake 8: Writing Numbers Wrong (Especially Phone Numbers & Dates)

**What happens:**
- Question: "What is the contact number?"
- Speaker: "Call 555-0-1-2-3"
- You write: "5550123" (missing one digit) or "55501213" (added one)
- 0 points.

**Why:** Numbers are read FAST. You're not concentrating.

**Fix:**
- During pre-reading: If question mentions "phone," "number," "date," "price," be ready to write slowly and carefully.
- Repeat the number back to yourself (mentally) as you write it.
- Double-check: If they say "0-2-7" and you wrote "27," you're missing the "0".

**Common number mistakes:**
- Phone: "555 0-1-2 3" → You wrote "5550123" but it's "5550123" (with dashes or spaces)
- Date: "23rd September" → You wrote "23/9" but they want "September 23rd"
- Price: "£250" but you wrote "250" (forgot the currency symbol)

**Practice:** In next full test, mark every number question. After test, check only the numbers. Did you get them exactly right?

### Mistake 9: Getting Stuck on One Difficult Question and Missing the Next 3

**What happens:**
- Question 8: "What qualification does the director NOT have?"
- You didn't hear the answer clearly
- You spend 30 seconds rewinding mentally, trying to remember
- Questions 9, 10, 11 go by while you're stuck
- You miss 4 questions total

**Why:** You haven't learned to move on. Perfectionism costs you.

**Fix:**
- **Accept that you'll miss some.** Even Band 9 scorers miss 1-2 questions per test.
- **Develop a "move on" habit:**
  - If you miss an answer, write something (a guess). Move on immediately.
  - If you're unsure about an answer, mark it lightly. If you have TIME at the end, come back.
  - BUT: At exam pace, you won't have time at the end. So move on NOW.

**Example self-talk:**
- "I didn't catch that. Moving on." (0.5 seconds)
- Write your best guess (if it's multiple choice, pick "C" as default guess)
- Focus on the NEXT question

**Training:**
- In next practice test, set a timer: 30 seconds per question. When it beeps, move to next question. No exceptions.
- This trains your brain: "Spend 30 seconds max, then move on."

### Mistake 10: Not Reviewing Your Answers Properly (You Write the Right Word, But It's Incomplete or Spelled Wrong)

**What happens:**
- Question: "Name two types of accommodation."
- You write: "hotel" and "apartment"
- Examiner says: "apartment" is spelled "apartment," but you wrote "apartmant"
- 1 point instead of 2

**Why:** You rush through, don't check your spelling/completeness.

**Fix:**
- After each section (during pause): Glance at your answers. Are they complete? Spelled right?
- Use the pause time (30 seconds) to scan Section 1-2 answers quickly.
- Don't change answers unless you're sure. If you wrote "hotel," and you're not sure if it should be "Hotel," leave it.

## Section-Specific Mistakes

### Section 1 Mistakes (Sections Easiest to Fix)

**Mistake:** Forgetting to write the format the question asks for.
- Question: "Write the date in DD/MM/YY format"
- You wrote: "23 September 2024"
- Should be: "23/09/24"

**Fix:** During pre-reading, note FORMAT requirements. "What format?" for dates, phone numbers, etc.

---

### Section 2 Mistakes

**Mistake:** Confusing left and right on a map.
- Question: "What is to the LEFT of the building?"
- Speaker: "To the right of the building is the parking lot."
- You marked parking on the left.

**Fix:** During Section 2, carefully listen for "left," "right," "near," "opposite." These are map keywords.

---

### Section 3 Mistakes

**Mistake:** Attributing words to the wrong speaker.
- Question: "What does the tutor recommend?"
- Two speakers: Student and Tutor
- Student says: "I think we should focus on X"
- Tutor says: "I agree, but also consider Y"
- You write X (student's opinion, not tutor's recommendation)

**Fix:** Identify speakers immediately. "Hi, I'm Dr. Chen, the supervisor." Now you know: Dr. Chen's words = supervisor's view.

---

### Section 4 Mistakes

**Mistake:** The speaker mentions something, but it's an example, not the main point.
- Question: "What is the main concern about climate change?"
- Speaker: "For example, some people worry about glaciers melting. However, the main concern is rising sea levels."
- You wrote: "glaciers melting" (the example)

**Fix:** Listen for "for example," "such as," "specifically." These introduce examples, not main points. After "for example," the REAL point often follows.

## Quick Self-Diagnosis: Which Mistake Are You Making?

**Do this after your next full practice test:**

1. **Check Spelling:** Count how many answers are correct but misspelled. ← If >3, focus on Mistake 1
2. **Check Comprehension:** Count how many answers you didn't attempt. ← If >3, focus on Mistake 9
3. **Check Section Breakdown:** Did Section 4 score much lower than Sections 1-2? ← If yes, focus on Mistake 10
4. **Check Synonyms:** Did you miss questions where the speaker used a different word? ← If yes, focus on Mistake 5
5. **Check Contrast Words:** Did you miss answers that came after "however" or "but"? ← If yes, focus on Mistake 7

**Once you identify YOUR #1 mistake, focus 50% of your practice time on fixing it.**

For example:
- If Mistake 1 (spelling): Do spelling drills daily.
- If Mistake 9 (getting stuck): Do every practice test with a 30-second timer per question.
- If Mistake 5 (synonyms): List synonyms before each practice test.
