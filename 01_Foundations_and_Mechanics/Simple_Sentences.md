# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Simple Sentences</span>

## <span style="color: #00838f">What Is a Simple Sentence?</span>

> [!info] Core Rule
> One independent clause = One complete thought with subject and verb

**The Blueprint:**
```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│   Subject  +  Verb  +  Object      │
│                                     │
│   She     +  writes + reports      │
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

![[Pasted image 20260412012036.png]]

Simple sentences are direct, clear, and powerful. In IELTS, Band 8 essays use them strategically—often more than learners expect. This is the secret: sophisticated writing doesn't mean every sentence is complex.

---

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Why Simple Sentences Matter in IELTS</span>

When you write a topic sentence for a Task 2 paragraph, you use a simple sentence. When you describe a trend in Task 1 before adding details, you start with a simple sentence. Under timed pressure, simple sentences keep your writing accurate.

**Real-world IELTS examples:**

| Context | Example | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| Task 2 Topic Sentence | "Infrastructure failures disrupt daily life." | Direct, memorable, commands attention |
| Task 1 Anchor | "The city expanded bus routes last year." | Establishes clear facts before analysis |
| Timeless Facts | "Renewable energy costs have decreased." | Present simple carries authority |
| Speaking Foundation | "I work in finance." | Gives examiner clear context |

## <span style="color: #d4af37">Common Errors to Avoid</span>

### Error Type 1: Fragment with Subordinator

```
❌ WRONG: "Because public transport is unreliable."
   → Missing the main clause

✅ RIGHT: "Because public transport is unreliable,
          many workers arrive late."
```

### Error Type 2: Missing Main Verb

```
❌ WRONG: "Pollution from car exhaust affecting air quality."
   → "Affecting" is not a main verb here

✅ RIGHT: "Pollution from car exhaust affects air quality."
```

## How to Use Simple Sentences in IELTS Writing

**In Task 1 (Visual Description)**  
Start your paragraph with a simple sentence that makes the main point clear, then add complexity through additional sentences.

Example sequence:
1. Simple: "The graph shows coffee consumption rising over a decade."
2. Complex: "This increase, which was most pronounced in developing nations, reflects growing urbanization."
3. Simple: "By 2020, consumption had plateaued."

**In Task 2 (Essays)**  
Use simple sentences for topic sentences and thesis statements. They're memorable and forceful.

Weak topic sentence (too tentative): "It could be suggested that perhaps technology might have some benefits for education."

Strong topic sentence (simple): "Technology improves educational access in rural areas."

**In Speaking**  
When you're under pressure in Part 1 or 2, start with simple sentences, then expand. Your examiner understands you're thinking in real-time.

Speaking flow:
- Simple first: "I study engineering."
- Then expanded: "I'm studying civil engineering because I'm interested in infrastructure projects, and the university has a strong reputation."

## <span style="color: #00838f">The Progression: From Simple to Sophisticated</span>

**Two related ideas:**  
"The government changed policy. Bus reliability improved."

**Compound (equal importance):**  
"The government changed policy, **and** bus reliability improved."

**Complex (cause → effect):**  
"**Because** the government changed policy, bus reliability improved."

```
Simple        ══════════════════════════════╗
              Foundation (establishes facts)║
                                            ║
Compound      ══════════════════════════════╝  Equal relationships
              
Complex       ══════════════════════════════╗  Cause, condition,
              Higher sophistication         ║  explanation
                                            ║
Mastery       ══════════════════════════════╝  Use all strategically
```

Master simple sentences first—the rest flow naturally from there.
