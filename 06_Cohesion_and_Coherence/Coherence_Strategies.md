# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Coherence Strategies</span>

Coherence is logical organization. A coherent essay has one clear argument that develops logically from introduction through body paragraphs to conclusion. The reader can follow your thinking without confusion or backtracking.

Weak coherence is the most common reason for Band 6 essays on Band 8 ideas. The ideas are good—they're just organized poorly. Examiners note: "Ideas are not clearly sequenced" or "Paragraph connections are unclear." That's a coherence issue.

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">The T-E-E-L Paragraph Blueprint</span>

Every strong Task 2 body paragraph follows this structure:

**Topic sentence:** State your main point for this paragraph in one clear sentence.  
"Online education expands access for remote learners."

**Explanation:** Explain why/how this point is true.  
"Students in rural areas can now access quality instruction without traveling long distances."

**Example:** Provide realistic support—personal, general knowledge, or realistic scenario.  
"In Southeast Asia, many universities now offer live online courses that serve regional populations."

**Link-back:** Connect this point back to the essay question.  
"This accessibility advantage makes online education particularly valuable for developing nations."

> [!info] Rule
> One paragraph should carry one concept only. A second concept belongs in a new paragraph.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Essay-Type Coherence Mapping (Task 2)</span>

### Agree/Disagree

- Introduction: clear side.
- Body 1: reason one.
- Body 2: reason two.
- Conclusion: same side, no contradiction.

### Advantage/Disadvantage

- Direct AD: one paragraph advantages, one paragraph disadvantages.
- Outweigh AD: stronger side gets deeper development.

### Discuss Both Views

- Paragraph 1: View A logic.
- Paragraph 2: View B logic.
- Conclusion: your stance.

### Problem/Solution

- If asked causes and solutions, separate them strictly.
- If asked only solutions, do not spend a full paragraph on causes.

### Two-Part Question

- Answer Question 1 clearly in one section.
- Answer Question 2 clearly in the next.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Logical Progression Patterns</span>

- cause -> effect
- problem -> solution
- claim -> reason -> evidence
- past -> present -> future
- comparison -> evaluation

## <span style="color: #d4af37">Weak vs Strong Coherence Example</span>

❌ WEAK PARAGRAPH (Incoherent):
Online learning is popular. It is cheap. Teachers are important. Technology is changing. Students like phones.

✅ STRONG PARAGRAPH (Coherent):
Online learning has expanded because it reduces access barriers for remote learners. In many regions, students who previously traveled long distances can now attend live classes from home. This shift is particularly beneficial for low-income families, as it lowers transport and accommodation costs.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Coherence in Task 1 (Academic)</span>

- Opening: one-sentence paraphrase.
- Overview: biggest features only.
- Body 1 and 2: grouped comparisons, not random number listing.

Grouping models:

- highest vs lowest
- rise vs decline
- stable vs volatile categories

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Coherence Tricks Under Time Pressure</span>

1. Sentence-role labeling trick: Write TS, EXPL, EX, LINK in the planning margin.
1. One-line paragraph mission: If the mission is unclear, coherence will break.
1. Reverse test: Read the paragraph backward sentence by sentence; if sentence role is unclear, rewrite.

## 20-Second Coherence Scan

- One main idea per paragraph?
- Does each sentence support that idea?
- Is the example directly tied to the claim?
- Does the paragraph finish by linking back to the prompt?
