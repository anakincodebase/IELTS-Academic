# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Idioms for IELTS Speaking</span>

Yes and no.

Idioms are one type of idiomatic language. Idiomatic language includes:
- idioms
- phrasal verbs
- slang
- colloquialisms

These are forms you hear native speakers use, but they should not be forced.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Core Rule</span>
Using as many idioms as possible is not a good strategy.

Why:
1. Idioms are not appropriate in every answer.
2. Incorrect use lowers Lexical Resource and coherence.
3. Forced memorized lines sound unnatural.

Use an idiom only if:
1. It fits the exact context.
2. You can use it 100 percent accurately.
3. It sounds natural in your own speaking voice.

> [!info] Practical Insight
> Many high-band candidates use very few idioms. Quality beats quantity.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Band 9 Style Sample Answers (Natural, Not Forced)</span>

### 1) Why did you choose that career?
I did not initially want to study law, but my family strongly encouraged me. After one hospital visit, I realized medicine was not for me, so I decided to bite the bullet and choose law.

### 2) Why did you change careers?
Compared with legal practice, teaching English felt like a piece of cake in terms of qualification time. The income is lower, but the day-to-day interactions are much more positive.

### 3) Have you ever given jewelry to someone as a gift?
Yes, I bought my wife a diamond ring close to both her birthday and Christmas, so I could kill two birds with one stone.

### 4) Have you ever received jewelry as a gift?
Yes. My wife bought me a watch I wanted for years, but the bank alert on my phone let the cat out of the bag before the celebration.

### 5) Do you think you will have the same career in the future?
Probably not. With AI changes, it would be risky to put all my eggs in one basket.

### 6) How often do you go out with friends?
These days it happens once in a blue moon because family schedules are busy.

### 7) Do you think you changed as you got older?
Absolutely. I used to burn the candle at both ends, but now I still work hard without exhausting myself.

### 8) Do you own or rent your home?
We rent because it gives us mobility. We can relocate at the drop of a hat if needed.

### 9) What do you dislike about your job?
Some people fixate on mistakes and refuse to improve. There is no value in crying over spilt milk.

### 10) How often do you buy new clothes?
Not often. I believe you cannot judge a book by its cover.

### 11) What type of jewelry do you like best?
I prefer watches. I do not count my chickens before they hatch, but some have retained value over time.

### 12) Who does the cooking in your home?
My wife cooks most meals and always goes the extra mile with presentation and detail.

### 13) Do you like your neighborhood?
Yes and no. It is beautiful, but the weather can be harsh, sometimes raining cats and dogs.

### 14) Do you have a healthy diet?
I start well each day, but in the evening I sometimes throw in the towel.

### 15) Will you change careers in the future?
It is possible. I prefer to cross that bridge when I come to it.

### 16) Are you okay with someone taking your photo randomly?
Not really. Social rules vary by culture, so when in Rome, I try to understand local norms.

### 17) Is there anything you dislike about social media?
Platforms can improve quality but move slowly. Rome was not built in a day, but progress should still be faster.

### 18) How do you approach long-term goals?
I plan consistently and remind myself that Rome was not built in a day.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">IELTS-Safe Usage Checklist</span>
1. Maximum 0 to 2 idioms per full speaking test unless naturally required.
2. Avoid repeating the same idiom in different parts.
3. Do not use idioms that you cannot explain in plain English.
4. Prioritize clear grammar and logical development.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Quick Practice</span>
1. Pick 5 idioms.
2. Write one Part 1 answer and one Part 3 answer for each.
3. Replace any forced idiom with plain language.
4. Keep only idioms that still sound natural.
