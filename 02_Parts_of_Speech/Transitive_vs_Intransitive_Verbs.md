# Transitive vs Intransitive Verbs: The Complete IELTS Guide

**The Core Concept:**
Transitive verbs require an object—something that receives the action. Intransitive verbs don't. The difference matters because only transitive verbs can be made passive, and IELTS loves testing this distinction.

**The Simplest Test:**
After the verb, ask "What?" or "Whom?" If the question makes sense and is answerable, the verb is transitive. If the question doesn't fit, the verb is intransitive.

**Example:**
- "She raised her hand." (Raised what? Her hand. → Transitive)
- "Prices rose sharply." (Rose what? Doesn't fit. → Intransitive)

---

## Why This Matters for IELTS

**Writing Task 1:** Most trend verbs are intransitive. You describe changes that happen independently—stocks fell, unemployment rose, demand increased. Understanding which verbs are intransitive prevents errors like "Prices were risen" (wrong; you can't passivize intransitive verbs).

**Grammar Accuracy:** Using a transitive verb without an object creates a fragment. "The government implemented" (implemented what?) needs an object.

**Passive Voice:** Only transitive verbs have passive forms. Trying to passivize an intransitive verb is a grammar error that drops your score.

## Why This Matters in IELTS
- Task 1 trend verbs are often intransitive (rise, fall, decline).
- Passive voice is only possible with transitive verbs.

## The 20 Most Confusing Pairs in IELTS

Many students confuse similar-looking verbs. These are the pairs that appear repeatedly in IELTS:

### Group 1: Rise vs. Raise vs. Arise

**Rise (intransitive):** Move upward  
"Prices rose 15% last year." (Nothing receives the action)  
Passive form DOESN'T EXIST. ✗ "Prices were risen" is wrong.

**Raise (transitive):** Lift or increase something  
"The government raised taxes." (Taxes receive the action)  
Passive: "Taxes were raised by the government."

**Arise (intransitive):** Emerge, come into being  
"Problems arose during implementation." (No object)  
Cannot be passivized.

**IELTS Error Pattern:**  
✗ "The issue was risen in the meeting."  
✓ "The issue arose in the meeting." OR "The committee raised the issue."

---

### Group 2: Fall vs. Fell

**Fall (intransitive):** Go down  
"Sales fell by 20%." (Incomplete: "Sales were fallen"—wrong)  
Forms: fall, fell, fallen

**Fell (transitive):** Cut down, knock over  
"Loggers felled ancient trees." (Trees receive the action)  
Forms: fell, felled, felled (note: past tense "felled" ≠ past tense of "fall")

**IELTS Error Pattern:**  
✗ "The trees were fallen by the storm."  
✓ "The storm felled the trees." OR "The trees fell in the storm."

---

### Group 3: Lie vs. Lay

**Lie (intransitive):** Rest in a flat position, be situated  
"The town lies on the coast." (No object)  
"She lay in bed." (She receives no action; she's just positioned)  
Forms: lie, lay, lain

**Lay (transitive):** Place something down, put something  
"Workers laid the foundation." (Foundation receives the action)  
"She laid the papers on the table." (Papers receive the action)  
Forms: lay, laid, laid

**IELTS Error Pattern:**  
✗ "The responsibility was lain with the managers."  
✓ "Responsibility lay with the managers." OR "The managers laid out the plan."

**Memory trick:** You LIE down yourself. You LAY something down (it requires an object).

---

### Group 4: Sit vs. Set

**Sit (intransitive):** Be in a seated position  
"The village sits on a hillside." (No object)  
"She sat for the exam." (She's not receiving an action; she's in a position)  
Forms: sit, sat, sat

**Set (transitive):** Place, establish, arrange  
"The committee set new limits." (Limits receive the action)  
"They set the table." (Table receives the action)  
Forms: set, set, set

**IELTS Error Pattern:**  
✗ "The meeting was sat in silence."  
✓ "The meeting was held in silence." (NOT set/sat)  
✓ "They set ground rules for the meeting."

---

### Group 5: Increase/Decrease (Transitive) vs. Rise/Fall (Intransitive)

**Increase (transitive):** Make something bigger  
"The company increased production by 40%." (Production receives the action)  
"Production was increased." (Passive works)

**Rise (intransitive):** Go up on its own  
"Production rose by 40%." (No object; it happened independently)  
"Production was risen." ✗ (WRONG—can't passivize)

**Decrease/Fall (same logic):**  
Transitive: "They decreased costs."  
Intransitive: "Costs fell."

**IELTS Task 1 Reality:**  
Band 6 mistake: "Profits were risen."  
Band 8 version: "Profits rose" or "The company raised profits."

---

### Group 6: Affect vs. Effect

**Affect (transitive verb):** Influence  
"Climate change affects agriculture." (Agriculture receives the influence)  
"The policy affected employment rates."

**Effect (noun = result; rare as verb = cause to happen):**  
Noun: "The effect was immediate." (The result...)  
Verb (rare): "We will effect change." (We will CAUSE change—sounds formal/awkward)

**IELTS Context:**  
"Climate change affects [verb] crop yields."  
NOT "effects crop yields" (wrong spelling/grammar)

---

### Group 7: Improve vs. Improve + Object

**Improve (can be both transitive and intransitive):**  
Intransitive: "The situation improved." (Improved on its own)  
Transitive: "The government improved infrastructure." (Infrastructure receives the action)

**Key for IELTS:** You can use either, but precision matters.  
"Literacy rates improved" = rates went up on their own  
"Programs improved literacy rates" = programs made the change

## Real IELTS Task 1 Examples

**Band 6 (Wrong):**  
"The number of students were raised in 2015."

**Band 8 (Correct):**  
"The number of students rose sharply in 2015."  
OR  
"The institution raised enrollment in 2015."

## The Passive Voice Safety Test

**Rule:** Only transitive verbs can be passivized.

✗ Intransitive attempt: "The situation was happened."  
✓ Correct: "The situation happened."

✗ Intransitive attempt: "The prices were fallen."  
✓ Correct: "The prices fell."

✓ Transitive passive: "The policy was implemented." (works)

## 90-Second Editing Drill for Your Writing

1. Scan your essay. Circle every main verb.
2. For each verb, ask: "Does this need an object?"
3. If YES (transitive), verify an object follows. If NO, you have a fragment.
4. If the verb is a trend verb (rise, fall, decline, increase), verify it's intransitive.
5. Check any passive forms. Ask: "Is the original verb transitive?" If intransitive → error.

**Example Paragraph Check:**
"The government raised awareness through campaigns. The number of participants rose by 30%. This trend reflected changing attitudes. Authorities established new programs."

- "raised" → transitive (awareness = object) ✓
- "rose" → intransitive (no object) ✓  
- "reflected" → transitive (attitudes = object) ✓
- "established" → transitive (programs = object) ✓

All correct!

## Passive Voice Safety Check
- Active transitive: The committee approved the plan.
- Passive valid: The plan was approved.

- Active intransitive: Prices rose quickly.
- Passive invalid: Prices were risen quickly.

## IELTS Error Patterns
> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: The company was fallen by 15%.
>
> Correct: The company's revenue fell by 15%.

> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: The awareness was raised up quickly.
>
> Better: Awareness rose quickly.
>
> Also correct: The campaign raised awareness quickly.

## 90-Second Editing Drill
1. Circle every main verb in one paragraph.
2. Mark T (transitive) or I (intransitive).
3. Check that each T verb has a clear object.
4. Remove passive structures built from I verbs.
5. Replace weak verb choices with precise alternatives.
