# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Nouns</span>

Nouns are words that name things. A person (teacher), a place (city), an idea (freedom), a quality (courage). IELTS rewards writers who choose specific, precise nouns. Replacing a generic noun like "thing" with a specific noun like "infrastructure" signals clarity and sophistication.

In IELTS, noun errors often involve countable/uncountable confusion. "Many researches show" is wrong because research is uncountable. "Much research shows" is correct.

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Countable vs. Uncountable</span>

Countable nouns name things you can count individually. They take a/an, pluralize with -s, and use "many."

"One student, two students, many students."  
"A report, several reports."

Uncountable nouns name things you can't count as separate units. They don't take a/an, don't pluralize, and use "much."

"Much information" (not "many information")  
"Some advice" (not "an advice" or "advices")

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Nouns Inside Parts of Speech (Concept)</span>
Noun types are not one single list. A noun can belong to multiple categories at the same time.

Example:
- education is common + abstract + uncountable
- committee is common + concrete (institutional) + collective + countable

Think in 4 classification layers:
1. naming scope (proper/common)
2. meaning type (concrete/abstract/material)
3. grammar behavior (countable/uncountable/singular/plural/possessive)
4. structure (simple/compound/gerund/appositive use)

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Spelling Mnemonics for Plurals and Third-Person Singular Endings</span>

### <span style="color: #1b5e20">1. Hissing Rule</span>
If the word ends in a hissing or buzzing sound, add -es.

Endings:
- -s, -ss, -sh, -ch, -x, -z

Examples:
- pass -> passes
- wash -> washes
- watch -> watches
- fix -> fixes
- box -> boxes

### <span style="color: #1b5e20">2. Vowel Shield Rule (words ending in y)</span>
Check the letter before y.

- Vowel + y -> keep y, add s
  - play -> plays
  - boy -> boys
  - buy -> buys
- Consonant + y -> change y to ies
  - study -> studies
  - fly -> flies
  - cry -> cries

### <span style="color: #1b5e20">3. Heavy O Rule</span>
Many consonant + o words take -es.

Examples:
- do -> does
- go -> goes
- tomato -> tomatoes
- hero -> heroes

Common exceptions:
- photos, pianos, logos

### <span style="color: #1b5e20">4. Thief Rule (f / fe -> ves)</span>
Many nouns ending in f or fe become ves.

Examples:
- leaf -> leaves
- wife -> wives
- half -> halves

Common exceptions:
- roofs, beliefs, chefs

### <span style="color: #1b5e20">5. Everything Else Default</span>
If no special ending rule applies, add -s.

Examples:
- write -> writes
- book -> books
- dream -> dreams

### <span style="color: #5e35b1">Compound and Complex Sentence Examples (Endings)</span>
> [!example] Compound
> The teacher explains the rule, and the students write the corrected forms.

> [!example] Complex
> Because the word ends in a consonant plus y, she changes y to ies before she submits the worksheet.
