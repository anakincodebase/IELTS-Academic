# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Pronouns</span>

Pronouns replace nouns and noun phrases. Instead of saying "I like John and John is my friend," you say "I like John and he is my friend." The pronoun "he" replaces the noun "John" and avoids repetition.

For cohesion in IELTS writing, pronouns are essential. But every pronoun must clearly refer to one specific noun. Ambiguous pronoun reference confuses readers and lowers your Coherence and Cohesion score.

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">The Core Rule</span>

Each pronoun must map to exactly one clear noun (its antecedent). If a reader has to guess what your pronoun refers to, it's wrong.

Clear: "The government increased bus subsidies, and it helped low-income commuters."
(The pronoun "it" clearly refers to "the government.")

Ambiguous: "The government worked with transport companies, and they disagreed about implementation."
(Does "they" refer to the government or the companies? Unclear.)

Clear: "The government worked with transport companies, and the companies disagreed about implementation."
(Or: "...and they [the companies] disagreed.")

## <span style="color: #00838f">SOPP Roles</span> 
1. Subject -> does the action: I, we, he, she, they
2. Object -> receives the action: me, us, him, her, them
3. Possessive Adjective -> shows ownership before a noun: my, our, his, her, their
4. Possessive Pronoun -> shows ownership alone: mine, ours, his, hers, theirs

Mnemonic:
- SOPP -> Subject, Object, Possessive Adjective, Possessive Pronoun

## Memorization Mnemonics
1. First person
  - I -> Me -> My -> Mine (I own me)
  - We -> Us -> Our -> Ours (we share)
2. Second person
  - You -> You -> Your -> Yours (singular and plural forms are identical)
3. Third person
  - He/Him/His/His
  - She/Her/Her/Hers
  - It/It/Its/-
  - They/Them/Their/Theirs

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Advanced Theory and Key Rules (IELTS)</span>

### Core Concept: Antecedent Rule
A pronoun replaces a noun, and that noun is called the antecedent.

> [!info] Golden Rule
> It must be fully clear what each pronoun refers to. If the examiner must guess the reference of it or they, coherence drops.

Ambiguous:
- Teachers should help students because they have a lot of experience.

Clear:
- Because teachers have a lot of experience, they should help students.

### Academic Tone Ban List (Task 1 and Task 2)
| Pronoun | Status in IELTS Writing | Better Academic Choice |
|---|---|---|
| you, your | avoid in formal writing | individuals, people, citizens |
| I, me, my | limited use only in opinion framing (intro/conclusion) | objective phrasing in body paragraphs |
| we, us | limited; acceptable for humanity in general | society, governments, communities |

Examples:
- Weak: If you use public transport, you save money.
- Upgrade: If individuals use public transport, they save money.
- Weak: We need to stop polluting.
- Upgrade: Society must reduce pollution levels.

### Demonstrative Pronouns (Band 8 Cohesion)
Demonstratives point to ideas: this, that, these, those.

For stronger cohesion in Task 2, summarize a previous sentence with:
- Demonstrative + abstract noun

Examples:
- Basic: Global temperatures are rising. This causes ice caps to melt.
- Band 8: Global temperatures are rising. This phenomenon causes ice caps to melt.
- Band 8: Governments are taxing carbon emissions. This policy encourages green energy.

### Relative Pronouns (Complex Sentence Builder)
| Pronoun | Used For | IELTS Academic Example |
|---|---|---|
| who | people (subject) | Citizens who recycle should receive tax breaks. |
| which | things/animals | Fossil fuels, which are heavily polluting, must be phased out. |
| that | people/things | The policy that the government implemented was flawed. |
| whose | possession | Children whose parents work long hours often feel neglected. |
| whom | people (object, formal) | The candidates whom the panel interviewed were highly qualified. |

Which vs That comma rule:
- Use that for essential information (no commas).
  - The cars that use electricity are better for the environment.
- Use which for extra information (commas required).
  - Electric cars, which are becoming cheaper, are better for the environment.

### Indefinite Pronouns (Subject-Verb Agreement Trap)
These are grammatically singular and normally take singular verbs:
- everyone, everybody, everything
- anyone, anybody, anything
- someone, somebody, something
- no one, nobody, nothing
- each, either, neither

Common corrections:
- Error: Everyone have the right to free healthcare.
- Correct: Everyone has the right to free healthcare.
- Error: Neither of the solutions are perfect.
- Correct: Neither of the solutions is perfect.

### Impersonal Pronouns (Formal Academic Framing)
Use impersonal structures to avoid overusing I and we.

Dummy it patterns:
- It is widely argued that...
- It is undeniable that...
- It is imperative for governments to...

Formal one pattern:
- If one considers the economic impact, one must conclude that...

### Singular They (Modern Academic English)
When the person is singular but gender is unknown or irrelevant, singular they is standard.

Clunky:
- When a teacher is stressed, he or she may lose his or her patience.

Fluent:
- When a teacher is stressed, they may lose their patience.

### Pronoun Cheat Sheets by Task

#### Writing Task 1 (avoid repetition in comparisons)
- those for plural comparison:
  - The figures for solar energy were significantly higher than those for wind power.
- that for singular comparison:
  - The population of Tokyo is larger than that of London.
- former and latter after two references:
  - Japan and the UK saw increases in GDP. The former grew by 2%, while the latter grew by 5%.

#### Writing Task 2 (cohesive referencing)
| To Refer Back To | Useful Pronoun Phrase |
|---|---|
| a previously mentioned problem | this issue, this dilemma |
| a previously mentioned solution | this approach, this measure |
| a trend or change | this shift, this development |
| two items mentioned together | both of these, neither of these |

#### Speaking Part 3 (generalization)
- Most people feel overwhelmed by social media because they are constantly bombarded with information.
- It depends on the individual, but generally speaking, everyone has to find their own balance.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Key Pronoun Rules to Remember</span>
1. Pronoun reference must be unambiguous.
2. Keep academic tone in writing tasks; avoid conversational pronouns.
3. Use demonstrative + abstract noun for high-level cohesion.
4. Use relative pronouns accurately to build complex sentences.
5. Check subject-verb agreement after indefinite pronouns.
6. Use impersonal it and one for formal claims.
7. Use singular they when gender is unknown or irrelevant.
8. In Task 1, use that/those/former/latter to avoid repetition.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">High-Value IELTS Patterns</span>
- this issue, this trend, this approach, these measures
- that/those for comparison in Task 1

> [!example] IELTS Example
> The unemployment rate in City A was lower than that in City B.

## <span style="color: #d4af37">Common Errors</span>
> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: Everyone have equal access.
>
> Correct: Everyone has equal access.

Present-tense agreement reminder:
- Singular indefinite pronouns (everyone, each, neither) -> is / has / does
- Plural pronouns and nouns (they, both, many students) -> are / have / do

> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: Teachers help students because they are experienced. (ambiguous)
>
> Correct: Because teachers are experienced, they can better support students.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Task Application</span>
- Task 1: use that/those to avoid repetition.
- Task 2: use demonstrative + abstract noun for cohesion.
- Speaking Part 3: if you use it/they, name the noun first.
