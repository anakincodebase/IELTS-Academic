# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Verbs: The Core of Every Sentence</span>

Verbs are the engine of English sentences. Every complete sentence needs at least one. Without a clear verb, you have fragments. Without the right verb, your meaning becomes unclear. IELTS scores depend heavily on verb accuracy.

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Main Verbs vs. Helping Verbs</span>

To build error-free sentences, you need to know exactly what job a verb is doing in your sentence.

- **The Main Verb:** The core action (run, eat, establish) or state of being (is, seems, becomes). Every sentence MUST have one.
- **The Helping Verb (Auxiliary):** Words like is, am, are, do, does, has, have act as "helpers" to show tense or voice.

```
Example: "The government IS BUILDING a new hospital."
         ↑ helping verb    ↑ main verb
```

> [!info] The Golden Rule
> If a helping verb is the **only** verb in the sentence, it becomes the Main Verb.  
> Example: "The infrastructure **is** poor." (state of being)

---

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Transitive vs. Intransitive Verbs</span>

This is where many advanced learners lose points. **Transitive verbs** transfer action to an object. **Intransitive verbs** don't.

**Quick Test:** Say the subject and verb, then ask "What?" or "Who?"

- If the question needs an answer → **Transitive**
- If the question makes no sense → **Intransitive**

```
"The city council raised..." → Raised WHAT? The taxes. ✅ Transitive
"The sun rose..." → Rose WHAT? (doesn't make sense) ✅ Intransitive
```

---

### Common Confusing Pairs

**Rise (Intransitive) vs. Raise (Transitive)**

| Verb | Transitive? | Forms | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rise | No | rise, rose, risen | "Tourism **rose** significantly." |
| Raise | Yes | raise, raised, raised | "Governments should **raise** awareness." |

**Lie (Intransitive) vs. Lay (Transitive)**

| Verb | Transitive? | Forms | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lie | No | lie, lay, lain | "The responsibility **lies** with parents." |
| Lay | Yes | lay, laid, laid | "Workers **laid** the foundation." |

**Fall (Intransitive) vs. Fell (Transitive)**

| Verb | Transitive? | Forms | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fall | No | fall, fell, fallen | "Rates **fell** steadily." |
| Fell | Yes | fell, felled, felled | "They **felled** thousands of trees." |

## <span style="color: #d4af37">Non-Finite Verbs</span>

Finite verbs change with tense and subject. **Non-finite verbs do NOT change**—they become nouns, adjectives, or adverbs, allowing you to create sophisticated sentences.

### Infinitives (To + Base Verb)

Acts as subject or expresses purpose.

```
As subject:    "TO IMPLEMENT these changes requires funding."
As purpose:    "They built a dam TO GENERATE electricity."
```"

> [!warning] The "Bare Infinitive" Exception
> Certain phrases demand the verb without "to". The most common are *had better* and *would rather*.
> * **Correct:** "We had better **start** the project."
> * **Incorrect:** "We had better *to start* the project."

### B. Gerunds (Base Verb + ing)

Gerunds look like continuous verbs, but they act entirely like **nouns**. You use them to talk about the *concept* of an action.

* **Rule 1: Prepositions + Gerunds.** This is an unbreakable rule. If a verb comes immediately after a preposition (*in, on, at, for, about, of, from*), it **must** be a gerund.
  * **Correct:** "They are interested **in investing**."
  * **Incorrect:** "They are interested *in invest*."
  
* **Rule 2: Possessives + Gerunds.** Because gerunds act as nouns, you must treat them like nouns. 

* You wouldn't say "I don't mind *him* car"; you say "I don't mind *his* car." Therefore, use possessive adjectives (*my, your, his, her, our, their*) before gerunds.

  * **Correct:** "The manager objected to **their** leaving early."
  * **Incorrect:** "The manager objected to *them* leaving early."

### C. Participles (Verbs acting as Adjectives)

Participles describe nouns. Using them correctly shows high-level grammatical control.

* **Present Participle (V-ing):** Describes an active noun (the noun is doing the action).
  * *Example:* "The **rising** sea levels threaten coastal cities." (The levels are doing the rising).
* **Past Participle (V3):** Describes a passive noun (the noun had the action done to it).
  * *Example:* "The **abandoned** vehicles were towed away." (The vehicles were abandoned by someone else).
* **Perfect Participle (Having + V3):** Used at the beginning of a sentence to show that one complete action finished before the main sentence action began. This is a brilliant structure for IELTS Writing Task 2.
  * *Example:* "**Having recognized** the dangers of fossil fuels, many countries are now shifting to renewable energy."

> [!danger] Dangling Participle Error
> The subject of the participle clause MUST match the main clause.
>
> ❌ "Having finished the essay, the TV was turned on."  
> (The TV did not finish the essay ❌)
>
> ✔ "Having finished the essay, she turned on the TV."

## 4. Dynamic Verbs: The Architecture of Action

Dynamic verbs, inherently volitional and temporally bounded, articulate actions, processes, or transitional events. They possess a discernible beginning, middle, and end, making them inherently compatible with the progressive (continuous) aspect. In academic discourse, they are the engines of narrative and methodology.

### Sub-categories of Dynamic Verbs

* **Activities:** Prolonged actions without a definitive endpoint.
  * *Examples:* deliberate, investigate, synthesize.
* **Accomplishments:** Actions that culminate in a logical endpoint or result.
  * *Examples:* establish, resolve, construct.
* **Achievements:** Instantaneous transitions or punctiliar events.
  * *Examples:* discover, recognize, culminate.

### Advanced Usage & Context

> **Example 1 (Activity):** "Despite the profound bureaucratic hurdles, the international committee **is currently orchestrating** a multilateral intervention to stabilize the region."
> 
> **Example 2 (Accomplishment):** "By the time the symposium concluded, the researchers **had meticulously dismantled** the prevailing theories surrounding cellular senescence."

---

## 5. Stative Verbs: The Permanence of Condition

In stark contrast, stative verbs describe conditions, relationships, cognitive processes, or emotional states. Because they reflect intrinsic states rather than volitional actions, they are categorically resistant to the progressive aspect (i.e., they are rarely used with *-ing*). They denote permanence, or at least, an uninterrupted continuum.

### Sub-categories of Stative Verbs

* **Cognitive & Epistemic States:** Pertaining to knowledge, belief, and intellectual processes.
  * *Examples:* comprehend, presuppose, conceptualize, doubt.
* **Affective & Emotional States:** Pertaining to emotional dispositions.
  * *Examples:* loathe, revere, deplore.
* **Relational & Possessive States:** Denoting ownership, composition, or ontological relationships.
  * *Examples:* encompass, constitute, possess, entail.
* **Sensory Perception:** Involuntary reception of sensory data.
  * *Examples:* perceive, resonate, appear.

### Advanced Usage & Context

> **Example 1 (Cognitive):** "The author's overarching hypothesis inextricably **presupposes** that socioeconomic variables **correlate** deeply with longitudinal educational outcomes."
> 
> **Example 2 (Relational):** "This specific demographic cohort **constitutes** a profound anomaly within the broader statistical paradigm."

## 6. Lexical Ambiguity: Verbs with Dual Citizenship

The true hallmark of an advanced writer is the ability to navigate verbs that exhibit semantic fluidity, those that can function as either stative or dynamic depending exclusively on their contextual deployment. When a stative verb adopts a progressive form, its fundamental meaning inevitably undergoes a profound shift, usually transitioning from a permanent state to a temporary, volitional action.

### The Semantic Shift in Action

**To Be (State vs. Behavior)**

* *Stative (Inherent trait):* "The visiting professor **is** remarkably obtuse regarding modern pedagogical frameworks."
* *Dynamic (Temporary behavior):* "By refusing to acknowledge the counterarguments, the professor **is being** deliberately obtuse."

**To Think (Opinion vs. Cognitive Process)**

* *Stative (Firmly held belief):* "The scientific community largely **thinks** that carbon sequestration is a viable interim solution."
* *Dynamic (Active contemplation):* "The regulatory board **is currently thinking** about revising the stringent ethical guidelines governing AI research."

**To Have (Possession vs. Experience)**

* *Stative (Ownership):* "The institution **has** an unprecedented monopoly on rare archival manuscripts."
* *Dynamic (Experiencing/Undergoing):* "The delegates **are having** an increasingly contentious debate regarding the allocation of federal subsidies."

## 7. Verb Patterns (Critical for Accuracy)

Some verbs follow specific structures:

### A. Verb + Gerund

- avoid, enjoy, suggest, consider

*Example:* "Although the deadline was unrealistic, they avoided **making** claims that the dataset could not support."

### B. Verb + Infinitive

- decide, plan, hope, want

*Example:* "After the pilot failed in rural districts, they decided **to invest** in infrastructure that could scale nationally."

### C. Verb + Object + Infinitive

- allow, encourage, force

*Example:* "Because landfill capacity is shrinking, the government encouraged households **to recycle** more consistently, and municipalities expanded collection points."

✔ "suggest doing"

## 8. High-Level Verb Alternatives (Lexical Resource)

Avoid basic verbs like *do, make, get*.

| Basic | Advanced |
|------|--------|
| do research | conduct research |
| make a decision | reach a decision |
| get better | improve |
| make a problem | cause an issue |

*Example:*  
❌ "This policy will make problems for low-income households, and social tension will rise."  
✔ "This policy will cause significant issues for low-income households, which could widen social inequality."

## 8.1 Irregular Verb 

### A. The Lazy Verbs (same in all 3 forms)

These never change.

- cut - cut - cut
- hit - hit - hit
- let - let - let
- put - put - put
- read - read - read (spelling same, pronunciation changes: reed -> red -> red)

### B. The Boomerang Verbs (V1 = V3)

They change in V2, then return in V3.

- come - came - come
- become - became - become
- run - ran - run

### C. The Sing-Song Vowels (i - a - u)

Use the rhythm to remember.

- begin - began - begun
- drink - drank - drunk
- swim - swam - swum

### D. Catch and Buy Rhyme Family (-ought / -aught)

Past and participle match and share the same sound family.

- bring - brought - brought
- buy - bought - bought
- catch - caught - caught
- teach - taught - taught
- think - thought - thought

### E. Twin Verbs (V2 = V3)

Once you know V2, you already know V3.

- build - built - built
- send - sent - sent
- spend - spent - spent
- keep - kept - kept
- sleep - slept - slept
- find - found - found
- meet - met - met
- hold - held - held

### F. The -EN Endings (classic advanced irregulars)

These are crucial in perfect tenses and passive structures.

- break - broke - broken
- choose - chose - chosen
- eat - ate - eaten
- fall - fell - fallen
- forget - forgot - forgotten
- hide - hid - hidden
- take - took - taken
- write - wrote - written

### Practical Drill (Pattern-per-day Method)

Do not memorize the entire list at once. Pick one pattern each day, then write 3 real-life sentences.

Example: -EN day

- I have eaten breakfast before class.
- I have taken detailed notes for revision.
- I have not forgotten to practice speaking.

## 9. Subject-Verb Agreement

### What Is Subject-Verb Agreement?

In English, the verb must agree with its subject in both number (singular/plural) and person (first/second/third).

**Core rule:** subjects and verbs must agree in number.

### Subject-Verb Agreement Chart

| Subject Type | Correct Verb Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Singular subject | Singular verb | "The committee that oversees urban transport approves the annual budget." |
| Plural subject | Plural verb | "The committees that oversee urban transport approve the annual budget." |

### Basic Rules

1. **Singular subject -> singular verb**
  - "When the principal investigator revises the methodology, she is more likely to detect bias."
  - "Because the policy is ambiguous, the department that handles appeals becomes overloaded."
2. **Plural subject -> plural verb**
  - "When the students who commute long distances are exhausted, they are less likely to participate."
  - "The reports that summarize quarterly outcomes are published after external review."

### Special Cases

#### A. Compound Subjects

Subjects joined by **and** usually take a plural verb.

- "The finance minister and the central bank governor are coordinating a joint response."

If the two nouns refer to one single idea/entity, use a singular verb.

- "Slow and steady progress is often more sustainable than rapid expansion."

#### B. Subjects Joined by "Or" / "Nor"

With **or/nor**, the verb agrees with the nearest subject.

- "Either the lead analyst or the regional managers have to present the revised forecast before noon."
- "Neither the regional managers nor the lead analyst has enough data to defend that claim."

#### C. Indefinite Pronouns

Some are singular; some are plural; some depend on context.

- Singular: "Everyone who attended the policy forum is expected to submit a written response."
- Plural: "Few who opposed the amendment are willing to debate it publicly."

### Common Mistakes

#### 1. Misidentifying the Real Subject

- Incorrect: "The group of students from remote districts are requesting additional funding."
- Correct: "The group of students from remote districts is requesting additional funding."

#### 2. Ignoring Compound Subjects

- Incorrect: "The textbook and the workbook for this module is available online."
- Correct: "The textbook and the workbook for this module are available online."

### High-Value Tips for Accuracy

1. Identify the true subject first.
2. Decide singular or plural.
3. Match the verb to the subject, not nearby nouns.
4. Re-check special structures: **and**, **or/nor**, and indefinite pronouns.
5. Practice short correction drills daily.

### Confusing Words and Rules

#### A. Indefinite Pronouns (Usually Singular)

- anybody: "Anybody can join us."
- either: "Either option is fine."
- neither: "Neither of them is coming."
- one: "One of the students is absent."
- anyone: "Has anyone seen my keys?"
- everybody: "Everybody knows the answer."
- nobody: "Nobody wants to be late."
- somebody: "Somebody left a jacket behind."
- anything: "Is there anything else to discuss?"
- everyone: "Everyone needs to bring identification."

#### B. Quantifiers That Depend on Context

These follow the noun after **of**.

- "All the food was eaten." (uncountable -> singular)
- "All the books were sold." (countable plural -> plural)
- "Most of the cake was gone."
- "Some of the students are here."
- "None of the pizza was left." / "None of the students are absent."
- "Half of the team is here."
- "Part of the book is missing."

#### C. Prepositional Phrases (Do Not Change Agreement)

The subject still controls the verb, not the noun in the prepositional phrase.

- "The report on these projects is useful." (subject = report)
- "The effects of pollution are severe." (subject = effects)

Common prepositions that often appear between subject and verb:

- about, above, across, after, among, around, as, at

### Pronoun Agreement Note

Pronouns should agree with the noun they refer to.

- Formal IELTS style: "Everyone brought his or her own lunch."
- Modern neutral style: "Everyone brought their own lunch."

For IELTS writing, use one style consistently.

## 11. The "Ban List" (Upgrading Basic Verbs)

Examiners call words like *do, make, get,* and *show* de-lexicalized verbs because they carry very little precise meaning. To score Band 7+, replace them with specific academic verbs.

| The Basic Verb (Band 5) | The Academic Upgrade (Band 7.5+) | Example Context |
|---|---|---|
| make | formulate, generate, establish | "Governments must formulate new policies." |
| do | conduct, execute, undertake | "Scientists must conduct further research." |
| get | acquire, obtain, attain | "Graduates struggle to acquire employment." |
| give | provide, allocate, impart | "Schools should allocate more funding to sports." |
| show | illustrate, demonstrate, reveal | "The graph illustrates a sharp decline." |
| change | alter, modify, transform | "Technology has fundamentally transformed communication." |
| stop | cease, halt, eradicate | "We must eradicate extreme poverty." |

## 12. The Phrasal Verb Divide (Speaking vs. Writing)

A phrasal verb is a verb + preposition/particle (for example: *give up, look into, point out*).

- In Speaking: Use phrasal verbs to sound natural and fluent.
- In Academic Writing: Use phrasal verbs sparingly; prefer precise academic verbs when a formal alternative exists.

| Spoken Phrasal Verb (Use in Speaking) | Academic Translation (Use in Writing Task 1/2) |
|---|---|
| to look into a problem | to investigate an issue |
| to point out a fact | to highlight / emphasize a fact |
| to set up a business | to establish an enterprise |
| to get rid of waste | to eliminate / dispose of waste |
| to build up immunity | to accumulate / develop immunity |
| to find out the truth | to discover / ascertain the truth |

## 13. Active vs. Passive Voice (The Objective Tone)

In academic writing, the result is often more important than the doer. Passive voice removes the actor and creates a more objective tone.

- Active (focus on doer): "The government built the new hospital in 2020."
- Passive (focus on result): "The new hospital was constructed in 2020."

### When to Use Passive in IELTS

1. Task 1 process diagrams: "The tea leaves are harvested and then dried in the sun."
2. Task 2 generalizing claims: "It is widely believed that..."

---

## 14. Modal Verbs (The Art of Hedging)

Modal verbs (*can, could, will, would, should, may, might, must*) express certainty, ability, obligation, or possibility.

- Absolute (Band 5.5): "Video games cause violent behavior."
- Hedged (Band 8.0): "Excessive exposure to video games can potentially lead to aggressive behavior."

### Modals of Obligation (Task 2 Solutions)

- "Authorities must implement..." (strong necessity)
- "Policymakers ought to consider..." (strong recommendation)
- "Individuals could contribute by..." (possibility/suggestion)

## 15. The Danger Zone: Confusing Verb Pairs

### A. Rise vs. Raise

- **Rise (Intransitive):** Happens by itself; no object.
  - Correct: "Temperatures rose significantly."
  - Incorrect: "Temperatures were risen."
- **Raise (Transitive):** Someone raises something; takes an object.
  - Correct: "The government raised taxes."

### B. Affect vs. Effect

- **Affect (Verb):** To influence.
  - Correct: "Pollution deeply affects the environment."
- **Effect (Noun):** The result.
  - Correct: "Pollution has a terrible effect on the environment."

### C. Happen / Occur (Never Passive)

- Incorrect: "The accident was occurred at 5 PM."
- Correct: "The accident occurred at 5 PM."

## 17. Deep Dive: Transitive and Intransitive Verbs

The key question is simple: does the action transfer to an object?

### A. Transitive Verbs (Action Transfer)

- Formula: Subject + Transitive Verb + Direct Object
- What/Whom test: If you must answer "what?" or "whom?", the verb is transitive.
- Incorrect: "The city council implemented."
- Correct: "The city council implemented a new infrastructure policy."
- Correct: "The manager fired him."

### B. Intransitive Verbs (Standalone Action)

- Formula: Subject + Intransitive Verb (+ adverb/prepositional phrase)
- No direct object; the action stays with the subject.
- Correct: "The neglected bridge collapsed."
- Correct: "The power grid failed during the storm."
- Correct: "The citizens complained loudly to the mayor."

### C. Ambitransitive Verbs (Double Agents)

Some verbs can be both, depending on use.

- **Read**
  - Transitive: "She read the environmental report."
  - Intransitive: "She read quietly in the library."
- **Grow**
  - Transitive: "The government plans to grow the economy."
  - Intransitive: "The economy grew rapidly last year."

### Why This Matters for High-Level Writing

Only transitive verbs can become passive.

- Transitive (works):
  - Active: "The authorities repaired the roads."
  - Passive: "The roads were repaired by the authorities."
- Intransitive (cannot be passive):
  - Active: "The sun shone brightly."
  - Incorrect passive: "The sun was shone brightly."
  - Active: "The crisis happened yesterday."
  - Incorrect passive: "The crisis was happened yesterday."

## 18. Advanced Verb Patterns: Gerunds vs. Infinitives

### A. Core Concept

When two verbs appear together, the first verb (boss verb) controls the form of the second.

- Gerund: verb + ing
- Infinitive: to + base verb

Examples:

- "The government decided to ban plastic bags."
- "The government suggested banning plastic bags."

### B. Verbs Followed by Gerunds

| Boss Verb | Band 8.5 Academic Example | Incorrect (Band 6 Trap) |
|---|---|---|
| suggest / recommend | "Experts recommend implementing a carbon tax." | recommend to implement |
| avoid | "Citizens must avoid relying solely on private transport." | avoid to rely |
| consider | "The council should consider subsidizing public transit." | consider to subsidize |
| involve | "Solving this crisis involves restructuring the economy." | involves to restructure |
| risk | "Ignoring the issue means we risk damaging the ecosystem." | risk to damage |
| deny / admit | "The corporation denied polluting the local river." | denied to pollute |

### C. Verbs Followed by Infinitives

| Boss Verb | Band 8.5 Academic Example | Incorrect (Band 6 Trap) |
|---|---|---|
| tend | "People tend to consume more junk food when stressed." | tend consuming |
| fail | "Many policies fail to address the root cause." | fail addressing |
| attempt / seek | "Governments are attempting to regulate AI." | attempting regulating |
| intend / plan | "The city intends to construct a new railway." | intends constructing |
| refuse | "Some nations refuse to sign the climate agreement." | refuse signing |
| claim | "Opponents claim to have alternative solutions." | claim having |

### D. Golden Rule: Prepositions + Gerunds

If a verb follows a preposition (*in, on, at, about, for, of, by, with,* etc.), use a gerund.

- "The government is responsible for providing healthcare."
- "Society can benefit from investing in education."
- "They succeeded in reducing crime rates."
- "Students worry about failing exams."

### E. The "To" Trap (When "to" is a Preposition)

When *to* acts as a preposition, it must be followed by a gerund.

- "Many citizens object to paying higher taxes."
- "I look forward to discussing this further."
- "People are accustomed to having instant internet access."
- "Vehicle emissions contribute to worsening air quality."

### F. Verbs That Change Meaning

**Stop**

- Stop + gerund: quit an ongoing action.
  - "The factory stopped producing toxic waste."
- Stop + infinitive: pause to do another action.
  - "The workers stopped to eat lunch."

**Remember**

- Remember + gerund: memory of a past action.
  - "I remember visiting that museum as a child."
- Remember + infinitive: duty/task for the future.
  - "The government must remember to allocate funds."

**Regret**

- Regret + gerund: regret about a past action.
  - "He regrets dropping out of university."
- Regret + infinitive: formal bad-news structure.
  - "The authorities regret to inform the public that taxes will rise."

### G. Gerunds as Subjects (Nominalization Boost)

- Band 5.5: "If we tax junk food, it will improve public health."
- Band 8.0: "Taxing junk food would significantly improve public health."
- Band 5.5: "When they overused the land, it caused desertification."
- Band 8.0: "Overusing the land has directly caused desertification."

## 19. Causative Verbs (Have, Get, Make, Let, Help)

### A. The Art of Delegation

If you write, "The President built the new hospital," it means the President physically built it. To show arranged action (delegation), use causative verbs.

### B. Passive Causative (Band 8.5 Structure)

This is crucial for formal IELTS writing because it emphasizes results.

- Formula: Subject + have/get + object + past participle (V3)

Application in Writing Task 1 (maps/process changes):

- Band 6.0: "In 2010, the city council destroyed the old factory."
- Band 8.5: "In 2010, the city council had the old factory demolished."
- Band 8.5: "By 2020, the school had a new library constructed."

Application in Writing Task 2 (policy/administration):

- Band 6.0: "The government should test all the drinking water."
- Band 8.5: "The government must have all drinking water tested for contaminants."

### C. Active Causatives (Power and Control)

**Make (force/require)**

- Formula: make + person + base verb (no "to")
- Error: "Strict laws make citizens to recycle."
- Band 8.0: "Strict environmental laws make citizens recycle household waste."

**Have (assign responsibility)**

- Formula: have + person + base verb (no "to")
- Band 8.0: "The hospital administration had the senior doctors revise the emergency protocols."

**Get (convince/persuade)**

- Formula: get + person + to + base verb
- Error: "Advertising gets consumers buy products they don't need."
- Band 8.0: "Aggressive advertising campaigns get consumers to purchase products they do not genuinely need."

**Let (allow/permit)**

- Formula: let + person + base verb (no "to")
- Error: "Schools should let students to choose their subjects."
- Band 8.0: "Progressive education systems let students choose their own academic pathways."

### D. The "Help" Exception

Both forms are grammatically correct. Omitting "to" is usually more natural.

- Acceptable: "Subsidies help farmers to survive economic downturns."
- Preferred: "Subsidies help farmers survive economic downturns."

### E. Error Detection (Band 6 Traps)

| The Mistake | Incorrect (Band 5.5) | Correct (Band 7.5+) |
|---|---|---|
| Make + to trap | "The taxes make companies to leave." | "High corporate taxes make companies relocate." |
| Get + base trap | "The campaign got people stop smoking." | "The public health campaign got citizens to stop smoking." |
| Literal meaning trap | "The mayor planted 500 trees in the park." | "The mayor had 500 trees planted in the park." |

## 20. The Subjunctive Mood 

# Detailed Notes: The Subjunctive Mood

**Source:** Anglo-Link | English Grammar Lesson

## Introduction to the Subjunctive
The subjunctive mood is a specific verb form used to express:
* That something is important or recommended.
* A wish or a desire.
* An unreal, improbable, or hypothetical condition.

*Note on Regional Differences:* The present subjunctive is very common in American English. In British English, the present subjunctive is often replaced by an alternative structure, but the past subjunctive remains common (especially in conditionals and expressions like "I wish" or "if only").

---

## 1. The Present Subjunctive

The present subjunctive is primarily used in formal English after verbs and expressions that indicate recommendation, importance, or demand.

**Form:** It has the **exact same form as the base verb** (the infinitive without "to"). This form does not change for the third-person singular (no 's') and remains the same regardless of whether the main situation is in the present or the past.

**Trigger Expressions (Importance):**
* It is important...
* It is essential...
* It is vital...
* It is crucial...

**Trigger Verbs (Recommendations/Demands):**
* Suggest
* Recommend
* Propose
* Insist
* Demand

**Examples:**
* *Present context:* It is necessary that they **be** warned of the risks.
* *Past context:* It was vital that they **be** warned of the risks.
* *Using a verb:* I recommend that he **see** a specialist. (Not *sees*)
* *Past context:* I recommended that he **see** a specialist.

**Negative Subjunctive:**
Negative present subjunctive constructions are rare even in formal English. It is better to use a direct antonym. 
* *Instead of:* "It is important that she not be late."
* *Use:* "It is important that she be punctual."

### Alternatives to the Present Subjunctive
In British English (and informal spoken English generally), alternatives are often preferred:
1. **Using the modal "should" (British English preference):**
   * *Example:* It is necessary that they **should be** warned of the risks.
   * *Example:* I recommend that he **should see** a specialist.
2. **Using "for" + infinitive with "to":**
   * *Example:* It is necessary **for them to be** warned of the risks.
3. **Using normal tenses (Informal/Everyday English):**
   * *Formal:* It is necessary that they be warned...
   * *Informal:* It's necessary they **are warned**... (often dropping "that")
   * *Formal:* I recommended that he delay seeing...
   * *Informal:* I recommended he **delayed** seeing...

## 2. The Past Subjunctive

The past subjunctive is used to talk about **unreal, hypothetical, or improbable present or future situations**.

**Form:**
It has the exact same form as the **past simple tense**, with one major exception: for the verb "to be," the past subjunctive form is **"were" for all persons** (including *I, he, she, it*). 
* *(Note: In informal English, "was" is sometimes used for the 1st and 3rd person singular, but "were" is highly recommended to be grammatically safe).*

**Common Usage:**
**1. Conditional Type 2 (If Clauses):**
* Used for hypothetical situations.
* *Example:* If I **were** young, I would travel around the world. (Not *was*)
* *Example:* If I **didn't have** to do this, I would catch up on my reading.

**2. Other Expressions for Unreal Situations:**
* **It's time:** *It's time the Prime Minister **took** the matter in hand.*
* **I wish:** *I wish it **weren't** so gray today.*
* **If only:** *If only I **had** a faster computer.*
* **I'd rather:** *I'd rather you **didn't tell** everyone.*
* **Look / Behave / Sound + as if / as though:** *She behaves as if she **were** the last woman on earth.*

**Special Notes for Past Subjunctive Exceptions:**
* **"I wish" + "would":** If you want to indicate that you are annoyed by a current situation and want it to change, you can use "would" after "I wish." 
  * *Example:* I wish you **would stop** shouting at the TV.
* **Real/Probable Situations with "as if":** If a situation looks real or highly probable, you use normal present tenses rather than the subjunctive.
  * *Example:* She looks as if she **has heard** some great news. (Because it genuinely appears true).

## 21. 10/10 IELTS Execution Layer (Exam-Day Mastery)

### A. Top 20 Fatal Verb Errors Checklist

Use this list as a final scan before you finish Writing Task 1 or Task 2.

1. *suggest to do* -> use gerund: *suggest doing*
2. *recommend to implement* -> *recommend implementing*
3. *make somebody to do* -> *make somebody do*
4. *let somebody to do* -> *let somebody do*
5. *get somebody do* -> *get somebody to do*
6. *interested in invest* -> *interested in investing*
7. *contribute to worsen* -> *contribute to worsening*
8. *look forward to discuss* -> *look forward to discussing*
9. *The accident was happened* -> *The accident happened*
10. *The crisis was occurred* -> *The crisis occurred*
11. *A number of students is* -> *A number of students are*
12. *The number of students are* -> *The number of students is*
13. *People is* -> *People are*
14. *Data are* / *data is* -> keep one style consistently in your essay
15. *The graph show* -> *The graph shows*
16. *Having finished..., the TV was turned on* -> fix dangling participle subject
17. *The government insists that it stops* -> subjunctive: *that it stop*
18. *It is vital that policy is fair* -> subjunctive: *that policy be fair*
19. *Temperatures were risen* -> *Temperatures rose*
20. Repeating *do/make/get* everywhere -> replace with precise academic verbs

### B. 15-Minute Verb Upgrade Drill (Daily)

1. Write 5 basic sentences with *do/make/get/show*.
2. Upgrade all 5 with Section 11 alternatives.
3. Convert 3 active sentences into passive (only when focus is result/process).
4. Write 3 solution sentences using subjunctive frames from Section 20.
5. Write 2 causative sentences using *have/get + object + V3*.
6. End with a 60-second fatal-error scan using checklist A.

High score grammar is not long grammar.

## 22. Conjugation: IELTS Master-Guide

### 1. The Core Concept: Tense, Aspect, and Voice

When you conjugate an English verb, you adjust three systems:

- **Tense (Time):** past, present, future
- **Aspect (Status):** simple (fact), continuous (ongoing), perfect (completed before another time)
- **Voice (Focus):** active (subject performs action) or passive (action happens to subject)

### 2. Subject-Verb Agreement

This is one of the most common IELTS grammar weaknesses. Repeated agreement errors usually cap Grammar at Band 6.

**Core Rule:** In present simple, if the subject is singular (*he, she, it,* or a singular noun), the verb usually ends in **-s/-es**.

- **Everyone/Everything trap**
  - Error: "Everyone want better infrastructure."
  - Correct: "Everyone wants better infrastructure."
- **A number of vs. The number of**
  - *A number of* (= many) -> plural verb: "A number of students are struggling."
  - *The number of* (= statistic) -> singular verb: "The number of students is increasing."
- **Noun phrase trap**
  - Error: "The implementation of these new policies have failed."
  - Correct: "The implementation of these new policies has failed."

### 3. The Perfect Aspect (The Band 8 Necessity)

Examiners actively reward accurate present perfect use (**has/have + past participle**) because it shows time control.

- **Task 1 intro:** "Over the last decade, the percentage of renewable energy has increased significantly."
- **Task 2 context:** "In recent years, the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence has transformed the global economy."
- **Speaking experience:** "I have lived in this city for my entire life."

**Critical Trap:** Do not use present perfect with a finished past time marker (*in 2020, yesterday, last year*).

- Error: "The government has introduced the law in 2020."
- Correct: "The government introduced the law in 2020."

### 4. Passive Conjugation (The Academic Tone)

Academic writing often prefers passive structures for objective tone.

**Formula:** Subject + appropriate form of **be** + past participle

| Tense | Active (Informal) | Passive (Academic) |
|---|---|---|
| Present Simple | "The factory produces cars." | "Cars are produced by the factory." |
| Past Simple | "They built the bridge in 2010." | "The bridge was built in 2010." |
| Present Perfect | "They have solved the issue." | "The issue has been solved." |
| Future (will) | "The city will build a park." | "A park will be built." |
| Future (modal) | "The government should ban it." | "It should be banned." |

### 5. Conditional Conjugation ("If" Clauses)

#### Level 1: Core Conditionals

| Type   | When to Use             | Formula                                           | Example                                                |
| ------ | ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Zero   | General truths          | If + present -> present                           | "If it rains, the ground gets wet."                    |
| First  | Real future possibility | If + present -> will + base verb                  | "If we reduce emissions, temperatures will stabilize." |
| Second | Unreal present/future   | If + past simple -> would + base verb             | "If I had a million dollars, I would travel."          |
| Third  | Unreal past/regret      | If + past perfect -> would have + past participle | "If she had studied, she would have passed."           |

#### Level 2: Mixed Conditionals (Band 7.5+)

1. **Past action -> present consequence**
   - Formula: If + past perfect -> would + base verb
   - Frame: "If X had happened, Y would be different now."
   - IELTS example: "If the government had invested in public transport a decade ago, the city would not be so congested today."
2. **Present truth -> past consequence**
   - Formula: If + past simple -> would have + past participle
   - Frame: "If I were this type of person, my past would have been different."
   - IELTS example: "If I were a better swimmer, I would have joined them on the scuba trip yesterday."

#### Level 3: Inversion (Band 8.0 Style)

Use inversion to remove *if* and create formal tone.

| Mnemonic | Standard If-Clause | Inverted Form (Band 8+) |
|---|---|---|
| Had | "If we had known about the pollution..." | "Had we known about the pollution, we would have acted." |
| Were | "If I were the Prime Minister..." | "Were I the Prime Minister, I would change the law." |
| Should | "If you need further information..." | "Should you need further information, please ask." |

#### Level 4: Lexical Boost (Reduce Overuse of "If")

- **Provided that:** "The project will succeed provided that funding is secured."
- **As long as:** "Electric cars are beneficial as long as the power is renewable."
- **Unless:** "Pollution will worsen unless strict laws are passed."
- **Supposing:** "Supposing automation replaces manual labor, how will citizens earn a living?"
- **Even if:** "Even if taxes are raised, it might not fix the infrastructure."

#### Level 5: Exam Game Plan

1. Writing Task 2 (problem/solution): use third conditional to show missed prevention.
2. Writing Task 2 (conclusion): use *unless* or *provided that* for a strong final condition.
3. Speaking Part 2 (story): add one third conditional reflection.
4. Speaking Part 3 (abstract): use *supposing* for high-level hypotheticals.

### 6. Task-Specific Conjugation Cheat Sheets

#### A. Writing Task 1 (Mastering Time)

| Graph Time Range | Required Tense | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Entirely in the past (1990-2010) | Past simple | "The population grew steadily until 2005, when it peaked." |
| Past to present (2000-today) | Present perfect | "Since 2000, the figure has fluctuated dramatically." |
| Future projection (2030-2050) | Future passive/projection | "The population is projected to reach 10 million and is expected to plateau." |
| Process diagram (no dates) | Present simple passive | "First, the raw materials are collected and sorted." |

#### B. Writing Task 2 (Essay Tense Priorities)

- **Present simple:** for general truths
  - "Poverty forces many children out of education."
- **Present perfect:** for trends leading to now
  - "Sedentary lifestyles have become increasingly prevalent."
- **Present continuous:** for ongoing current change
  - "The wealth gap between the rich and the poor is widening at an alarming rate."

#### C. Speaking Part 2 (Narrative Tenses)

- **Past continuous** (scene-setting): "I was studying for my final exams when..."
- **Past simple** (main events): "...my computer suddenly crashed, and I lost all my data."
- **Past perfect** (backstory): "I was terrified because I had not saved my work for three hours."

## 23. Do-Support and Auxiliary Inversion (Questions, Negatives, Emphasis)

This is a core control area in English verbs. If there is no auxiliary verb in present simple or past simple, English uses **do / does / did** to build negatives, questions, and emphatic statements.

### A. Negatives in Simple Tenses

- Formula (present simple): Subject + do/does + not + base verb
- Formula (past simple): Subject + did + not + base verb

Examples:

- Correct: "Although the board appreciates innovation, it does not approve proposals that ignore ethical safeguards."
- Correct: "Because the baseline data were incomplete, the researchers did not publish the conclusion until external reviewers verified the model."
- Error: "Although the board does not approves proposals that ignore ethical safeguards, it claims to support innovation."

### B. Questions and Inversion

In questions, the auxiliary comes before the subject.

- Formula: Auxiliary + Subject + Base Verb ...?

Examples:

- "Do you believe that this solution will remain practical once emergency funding is withdrawn?"
- "Does the revised chart show a steady increase, or does it merely reflect seasonal volatility?"
- "Did the committee approve the budget after the legal team raised compliance concerns?"

### C. When Not to Use Do-Support

If a sentence already has an auxiliary or modal, do not add **do/does/did**.

- Correct: "Although the team is under pressure, they are not prepared to release unverified findings."
- Incorrect: "Although the team does not are under pressure, they are delaying publication."
- Correct: "She cannot attend the meeting because she is presenting the annual report to the ethics board."
- Incorrect: "She does not can attend the meeting because she presents the annual report."

### D. Emphatic Do for Strong Claims

Use emphatic **do/does/did** to add force or contrast.

- "I do acknowledge that automation can increase efficiency, but it may also displace vulnerable workers."
- "The indicators did improve after the intervention, although regional disparities remained substantial."

IELTS use: employ this sparingly for controlled emphasis, especially in Speaking Part 3 and selected Task 2 arguments.

### E. High-Frequency Error Traps

1. After do/does/did, always use the base verb.
  - Error: "He does not likes online learning when the platform is unstable."
  - Correct: "He does not like online learning when the platform is unstable."
2. Keep tense on the auxiliary, not the main verb.
  - Error: "Did she went to the hearing after the auditor filed objections?"
  - Correct: "Did she go to the hearing after the auditor filed objections?"
3. Avoid double auxiliaries.
  - Error: "Do you can explain why emissions rose although production fell?"
  - Correct: "Can you explain why emissions rose although production fell?"

## 25. Band 8+ Compact Verb Upgrade List

Use this section to upgrade lexical resource without memorizing huge lists. Each row gives you three high-value verbs and one model sentence with compound/complex structure.

| Purpose | High-Impact Verbs | Band 8+ Model Sentence |
|---|---|---|
| Make a problem worse | exacerbate, aggravate, compound | "Although inflation had already strained household budgets, the sudden tax increase further **exacerbated** inequality, and social trust declined." |
| Reduce a problem | mitigate, alleviate, ameliorate | "If local councils expand green transport, they can **mitigate** traffic congestion while they also reduce long-term healthcare costs." |
| Cause something to happen | trigger, prompt, precipitate | "Because housing costs rose faster than wages, the policy shift **triggered** migration from city centers to peripheral districts." |
| Put policy into action | implement, enforce, enact | "Unless authorities **enforce** emission limits consistently, even well-designed climate laws will remain ineffective." |
| Support an argument | advocate, endorse, champion | "Many economists **advocate** targeted subsidies because they protect vulnerable groups, whereas blanket subsidies distort markets." |
| Oppose an argument | challenge, refute, denounce | "While some commentators defended the proposal, independent researchers **refuted** its assumptions after they reviewed longitudinal data." |
| Study evidence deeply | examine, evaluate, scrutinize | "Before the committee approves funding, it must **scrutinize** the methodology so that preventable errors do not enter national policy." |
| Show data clearly | illustrate, demonstrate, reveal | "The latest chart **reveals** that productivity rose in urban sectors, but it also shows that rural wages stagnated." |
| Increase strongly | escalate, surge, proliferate | "As speculative investment intensified, property prices **surged**, and first-time buyers were pushed out of the market." |
| Decrease strongly | plummet, decline, diminish | "After stricter regulation was introduced, counterfeit imports **declined**, although informal distribution networks still persisted." |

Tip for IELTS Task 2: choose one verb family per paragraph and reuse it accurately with different structures instead of forcing too many rare verbs.
