# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Prepositions</span>

Prepositions show relationships between words. They connect a noun or pronoun to other parts of the sentence. Prepositions express relationships of time ("in May"), place ("in the city"), direction ("toward the goal"), and abstract connection ("interested in learning").

In IELTS writing, preposition errors are frequent and lower scores noticeably. A student might write "discuss about" instead of "discuss." Or "married with" instead of "married to." These seem small, but they add up and reduce your Grammatical Range and Accuracy score.

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Time Prepositions</span>

For specific times, use **at**: "at 6:00 PM," "at noon," "at midnight."  
For specific days and dates, use **on**: "on Monday," "on January 15."  
For months, years, and longer periods, use **in**: "in March," "in 2024," "in winter."

## <span style="color: #00838f">Form and Structure Rules</span>

### Core grammar formula
| Structure | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| preposition + noun phrase | prep + noun | in the city, at night |
| preposition + pronoun | prep + object pronoun | with him, for them |
| preposition + gerund | prep + V-ing | interested in learning |

> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: She is good in cook.
>
> Correct: She is good at cooking.

### Word order and position
- Preposition usually comes before its object.
- In formal style, avoid unnecessary preposition stranding when possible.

Examples:
- Informal: Who are you talking to?
- Formal: To whom are you talking?

## <span style="color: #00838f">Time Prepositions (Master Rule Chart)</span>

### in, on, at for time
| Preposition | Use | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| at | exact time points | at 6:00, at noon, at midnight |
| on | days and dates | on Monday, on 12 May |
| in | months/years/periods | in March, in 2026, in winter |

### for, since, from, by, until, during
| Form | Core Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| for + duration | length of time | for two years |
| since + starting point | start time to now/reference point | since 2019 |
| from ... to/until | start and end | from June to August |
| by + deadline | no later than | by 2030 |
| until/till | up to a point | until Monday |
| during + noun period | within period | during the lecture |

### in vs within
| Form | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| in + time period | after that period | The project will finish in two weeks. |
| within + time limit | any time before limit | Submit within two weeks. |

### in, on, at for place
| Preposition | Use | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| at | point/location | at home, at school, at the station |

### Position contrasts
| Pair | Core Difference | Example |
|---|---|---|
| above vs over | higher position vs covering/movement | above sea level, over the city |
| below vs under | lower position vs directly beneath | below average, under the bed |
| between vs among | two items vs more than two | between A and B, among students |
| in front of vs before | physical position vs order/time/formal | in front of the house, before lunch |

## <span style="color: #00838f">Prepositions with Adjectives</span>

Prepositions can sometimes appear after adjectives to complete or elaborate on the ideas or emotions the adjective describes. Prepositions used in this way are known as adjective complements. The preposition always comes directly after the adjective and is typically followed by a noun or gerund to form a prepositional phrase.

**Common prepositions used with adjectives:**
of, to, about, for, with, at, by, in, from

### Rules & Patterns
- There are no fixed rules for combining adjectives with prepositions, but some patterns exist.
- Synonymous adjectives often take the same prepositions (e.g., afraid/scared/terrified of).
- Antonymous adjectives also often use the same prepositions (e.g., smart/stupid of).
- Sometimes, the adjective and its noun form share the same preposition (e.g., interested in / interest in), but not always (fond of / fondness for).
- The best way to learn is to memorize common pairings.

### Synonymous Adjectives
Synonyms usually take the same preposition:
- afraid of / scared of / terrified of
  - "Megan was afraid of the thunderstorm."
  - "Megan was scared of the thunderstorm."
  - "Megan was terrified of the thunderstorm."

But some similar adjectives use different prepositions:
- upset about / displeased with / hurt by
  - "Philip is upset about what was said."
  - "Philip is displeased with what was said."
  - "Philip is hurt by what was said."

### Antonymous Adjectives
Antonyms often use the same preposition:
- smart of / stupid of
  - "It was smart of him to go on vacation."
  - "It was stupid of him to go on vacation."
- good at / bad at
  - "She’s good at skateboarding."
  - "She’s bad at skateboarding."

### Noun Forms with Prepositions
Adjectives and their noun forms often share prepositions:
- interested in / interest in
  - "I am interested in astronomy." / "I have an interest in astronomy."
- addicted to / addiction to
  - "He is addicted to playing tennis." / "He has an addiction to playing tennis."
- obsessed with / obsession with
  - "Julie is obsessed with that movie." / "Julie has an obsession with that movie."

But not always:
- fond of / fondness for
  - "He is fond of animals." / "He has a fondness for animals."

### Examples of Common Pairings

#### Adjective + of
afraid of, frightened of, scared of, terrified of
  - "Sam is afraid of dogs."
  - "Many kids are frightened of clowns."
  - "Are you scared of airplanes?"
  - "The poor baby was terrified of her crib."
kind of, nice of, sweet of, thoughtful of
  - "How kind of you to come early."
  - "That was nice of your sister to treat us to dessert."
  - "It’s very sweet of John to send a gift."
  - "It is thoughtful of passengers to thank their drivers."
odd of, strange of
  - "How odd of that man to wear sunglasses inside."
  - "It’s strange of you to change your mind like that."
proud of, rude of, smart of, sick of, tired of, silly of, stupid of
  - "Mom told me she is proud of my accomplishments."
  - "I thought it rude of her to interrupt me."
  - "That’s very smart of you."
  - "I am so sick of doing laundry every week."
  - "Olivia confessed that she is tired of dating Mike."
  - "It was silly of me to assume I was right."
  - "How stupid of that boy to drop out of high school."

#### Adjective + to
accustomed to, addicted to, committed to, dedicated to, devoted to, friendly to, good to, kind to, nice to, married to, mean to, rude to, unfriendly to, opposed to, similar to
  - "He quickly became accustomed to city life."
  - "I think I'm addicted to action movies."
  - "She is committed to the art of dance."
  - "How dedicated to your studies are you?"
  - "Lucy is devoted to her family."
  - "Henry is friendly to everyone."
  - "Was she good to you?"
  - "You should always be kind to others."
  - "Mrs. Roberts was nice to the cashier."
  - "Cassie is married to Nick."
  - "Don’t be mean to your classmates."
  - "The couple was rude to the waitress."
  - "A lot of cats are unfriendly to humans."
  - "I am opposed to these changes."
  - "His idea is similar to mine."

#### Adjective + about
angry about, furious about, mad about, anxious about, nervous about, stressed about, worried about, excited about, happy about, sad about, depressed about, sorry about, upset about
  - "My neighbor is angry about the loud music we played last night."
  - "That woman is furious about having to wait in line."
  - "You’re always mad about something."
  - "Joe is anxious about studying abroad next semester."
  - "She seemed nervous about the test."
  - "Rachel is stressed about finding a job."
  - "I am excited about a new book that’s coming out soon."
  - "Aren’t you happy about the way things turned out?"
  - "She might be sad about her grades."
  - "Bobby was depressed about his parents’ divorce."
  - "I’m sorry about yesterday."
  - "He’s probably upset about losing the soccer match."

#### Adjective + for
famous for, known for, responsible for, bad for, happy for, hopeful for
  - "Texas is famous for rodeos."
  - "Australia is known for its large kangaroo population."
  - "Coach Brown is responsible for the football team."
  - "Marianne feels bad for her coworker."
  - "Kyle is happy for his brother."
  - "The company felt hopeful for the future."

#### Adjective + with
angry with, furious with, annoyed with, fed up with, bored with, content with, fine with, OK with, disappointed with, displeased with, pleased with
  - "Nicole is angry with her mother."
  - "I heard he’s furious with you!"
  - "I’m annoyed with this traffic."
  - "She’s fed up with having to clean toilets all day."
  - "It’s hard not to be bored with long lectures."
  - "Taylor was content with moving to Chicago."
  - "I’m fine with having to rewrite the introduction."
  - "Are you sure you’re OK with this?"
  - "Molly seems disappointed with her performance."
  - "The boy’s parents are displeased with his behavior."
  - "We are very pleased with the number of donations we received."

With may also be used in passive sentences to describe states:
  - "Paris is especially crowded with tourists in the summertime."
  - "The cake is filled with fresh blueberries."
  - "The piñata is stuffed with hundreds of pieces of candy."

#### Adjective + at/by
amazed at/by, astonished at/by, shocked at/by, surprised at/by
  - "The man was amazed at/by the number of people offering to help him."
  - "She was astonished at/by the movie’s poor ratings."
  - "I’m shocked at/by his behavior."
  - "Flight attendants were surprised at/by the plane’s abrupt landing."

At can also describe skills and abilities:
  - "James is good at playing the guitar." (correct)
  - "I’m terrible at golf."

#### Adjective + in
interested in, involved in
  - "Bernard is interested in joining the school band."
  - "Is she involved in politics?"

#### Adjective + from
different from, protected from, tired from
  - "Turquoise is different from blue."
  - "We were protected from the storm."
  - "I became tired from studying all night."

### Adjectives with Multiple Prepositions
Some adjectives can be paired with multiple prepositions, sometimes with a change in meaning:
- "She’s very sorry about the mistake." / "She’s very sorry for the mistake."
- "Mark is disappointed with the decision." / "Mark is disappointed in the decision."
In some cases, prepositions are not interchangeable:
- "I’m so happy for them." (people)
- "I’m so happy about the wedding." (thing/event)
- "Dad is angry with Elizabeth." (person)
- "Dad is angry about Elizabeth’s sneaking out." (gerund)

For wider lexical pairings, use [[Collocations]].

## 9) Zero-Preposition and Omission Rules
- No preposition before:
  - home, downtown, upstairs, abroad
  - last year, next week, this morning, every day

Examples:
- Incorrect: I went to home.
- Correct: I went home.

- Incorrect: We met on last Friday.
- Correct: We met last Friday.
