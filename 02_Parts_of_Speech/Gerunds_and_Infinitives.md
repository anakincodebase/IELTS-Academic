# Gerund vs. Infinitive

When you put a verb after another verb (without conjugating it with tense), English gives you three choices: infinitive with "to" (to go), infinitive without "to" (go), or gerund (going).

Getting this right is critical in IELTS. Many errors come from using the wrong form. "I enjoy to read" is wrong. "I enjoy reading" is right. "I must to go" is wrong. "I must go" is right.

**General rule:** If you're completely unsure, the infinitive with "to" is the most common and statistically your safest guess.

---

## When to Use the Base Verb (Without "to")

Use the base verb after all modal verbs: can, could, may, might, must, should, will, shall, would.

Incorrect: "I must to go now."  
Correct: "I must go now."

Incorrect: "She can to speak French."  
Correct: "She can speak French."

Also use the base verb after specific expressions: let, make, would rather, had better.

Example: "The presenter made us all stand up." (Not "to stand up")

---

## 1. When to use the Base Verb (`do` without 'to')

You must use the bare infinitive in three distinct situations:

**A. After all Modal Verbs**
* *can, could, may, might, must, should, will, shall, would*
* *Incorrect:* I must to go now.
* *Correct:* I **must go** now.

**B. After specific common verbs and expressions**
* *let, make, would rather, had better, why not (in questions)*
* *Example:* The presenter **made** us all **stand up**. (Not *to stand up* or *standing up*).
* *Example:* **Why not go**? (Not *Why not going?*).

**C. After expressions that naturally end in "to"**
Because these expressions already end in "to", you simply follow them with the base verb. 
* *ought to, have to (obligation), used to (past habit), going to (future)*
* *Example:* You **ought to be** more cautious.

---

## 2. When to use the Gerund (`doing`)

**A. As the Subject of a Sentence**
When a verb acts as the subject of another verb, you must use the gerund (the noun form).
* *Incorrect:* To walk in nature is healthy.
* *Correct:* **Walking** in nature is healthy.
* *Exception:* If you start the sentence with a dummy subject "It is...", you revert to the infinitive: *It is healthy **to walk** in nature.*

**B. After a Preposition**
Any verb following a preposition (*for, in, on, about*, etc.) must take the `-ing` form.
* *Example:* I apologize **for being** late.
* *Phrasal Verbs:* Since phrasal verbs end in prepositions, they strictly take gerunds. (e.g., I **carried on reading**).
* *The "To" Trap:* Sometimes "to" acts as a preposition, not an infinitive marker. The most common example is *to be used to* or *to be accustomed to*. 
    * *Example:* He is used **to working** in a noisy office.
* *Important Note on Purpose:* Never answer a "Why?" question with "for + doing". Always use the infinitive of purpose. (e.g., *Why are you studying English?* $\rightarrow$ **To improve** my prospects. NOT *For improving...*).

**C. After specific common verbs and phrases**
Commit these to memory over time:
* *risk, avoid, recommend, suggest, stop, finish, enjoy, mind, consider, quit, keep, involve*
* *Phrases:* *can't stand, can't help, have difficulty, spend/waste time*
* *Example:* He **risks losing** his place in the finals.

**D. With activities using "go"**
* *Example:* I could have **gone jogging** yesterday. (Not *gone to jog*).

---

## 3. Verbs that can take BOTH (Infinitive or Gerund)

**A. No change in meaning**
* *start, begin, continue*
* *Example:* We started to climb / We started climbing. (Both are perfectly correct and mean the same thing).

**B. General Preference Verbs (like, hate, prefer)**
* To achieve a natural British speaking style, use the **gerund** when expressing a general, ongoing preference. (American English frequently defaults to the infinitive).
    * *Example (British preference):* I **like swimming**. I **hate waiting**.
* **Crucial Exception:** If you add "would" to these verbs to express a specific, immediate situation (*would like, would hate*), you **MUST** use the infinitive. 
    * *Example:* I **would hate to interrupt** you. (Not *would hate interrupting*).

**C. Complete CHANGE in meaning**
* **Remember:**
    * *+ Infinitive:* A future task you must not forget. (e.g., I must **remember to put** my glasses in my bag.)
    * *+ Gerund:* Recalling a memory from the past. (e.g., I **remember putting** my glasses in my bag last night.)
* **Regret:**
    * *+ Infinitive:* Feeling sorry about an action you are *about to take* now. (e.g., I **regret to tell** you this, but...)
    * *+ Gerund:* Feeling sorry about an action you *already took* in the past. (e.g., I **regret telling** her that joke.)

---

## 4. Gerund vs. Present Participle
Do not confuse gerunds with present participles just because they both end in `-ing`. 
* **Gerund:** Acts as a noun. (*Walking* is healthy).
* **Present Participle:** Used to form continuous tenses (*It is becoming popular*) or as an adjective (*The walk is energizing*).

## Common Error Traps
> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: I suggest to increase public transport.
>
> Correct: I suggest increasing public transport.

> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: They decided improving the system.
>
> Correct: They decided to improve the system.

> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: We discussed to build a new road.
>
> Correct: We discussed building a new road.

## Mnemonic Bank
- PREP + ING: after prepositions, use gerund.
- SUGGEST + ING: suggest, recommend, avoid usually take gerund.
- PLAN + TO: decide, plan, hope usually take infinitive.
- MODAL = BASE: modals take bare infinitive.
