# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Direct and Indirect Speech</span>

Direct speech reports someone's exact words in quotation marks. "He said, 'I am tired.'"  
Indirect speech reports the meaning without quotation marks. "He said that he was tired."

In IELTS academic writing, indirect speech is preferred because it sounds formal and objective. In Speaking Part 3, you often report ideas indirectly: "Critics argue that technology has reduced human connection." That's better than: "Critics say, 'Technology has reduced human connection.'"

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Key Changes When Shifting to Indirect Speech</span>

**Tense shifts backward:** present becomes past, past becomes past perfect.  
Direct: "I have finished the report," she said.  
Indirect: She said that she had finished the report.

**Pronouns shift based on context:**  
Direct: "I am tired," he said.  
Indirect: He said that he was tired. ("I" becomes "he" because the subject changed)

**Time expressions shift:**  
Direct: "I will see you tomorrow," she said.  
Indirect: She said that she would see me the next day.

**No quotation marks in indirect speech.** No commas before reported clauses (usually).

Punctuation notes:
- Direct speech usually has a comma before the quote.
- Indirect speech does not use quotation marks.
- In indirect speech, that is often optional after say + clause and tell + object + clause.
- After formal verbs like complain, explain, object, point out, protest, keeping that is better.

## <span style="color: #00838f">IELTS Core Principle</span>
> [!info] Rule
> In Writing Task 2 and Speaking Part 3, report ideas indirectly unless a direct quote is absolutely necessary.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Master Conversion Checklist</span>
When converting direct to indirect speech, check these in order:
1. Reporting verb (say, tell, ask, suggest, warn, etc.)
2. Pronouns and possessive adjectives
3. Tense backshift (if needed)
4. Time and place expressions
5. Sentence type (statement, question, command, exclamation)

## <span style="color: #00838f">Pronouns and Determiners</span>

### Pronoun shift
First and second person shift according to speaker-listener context, not automatically to third person.

Examples:
- Direct: She said, "He is my son."
- Indirect: She said that he was her son.
- Direct: "I am ill," she said.
- Indirect: She said that she was ill.

### This and these
1. In time expressions, this usually becomes that.
2. As determiners, this/these often become the.
3. As pronouns, this/these can become it/they/them.

Examples:
- Direct: She said, "She is coming this week."
- Indirect: She said that she was coming that week.
- Direct: He said, "I bought this pearl for my mother."
- Indirect: He said that he had bought the pearl for his mother.
- Direct: He said, "I found these beside the king's bed."
- Indirect: He said that he had found them beside the king's bed.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Time and Place Shifts</span>

### Time expression table
| Direct | Indirect |
|---|---|
| today | that day |
| yesterday | the day before |
| the day before yesterday | two days before |
| tomorrow | the next day / the following day |
| the day after tomorrow | in two days' time |
| next week/year | the following week/year |
| last week/year | the previous week/year |
| a year ago | a year before / the previous year |
| now | then |

Examples:
- Direct: "I saw her the day before yesterday," he said.
- Indirect: He said that he had seen her two days before.
- Direct: "I will do it tomorrow," he promised.
- Indirect: He promised that he would do it the next day.

### Same-day exception
If speech is reported on the same day, time change may be unnecessary.

Example:
- Direct: At breakfast he said, "I will be very busy today."
- Indirect: At breakfast he said that he would be very busy today.

### Place shift
Here can become there, but often a clearer phrase is better.

Example:
- Direct: She said, "You can sit here, Tom."
- Indirect: She told Tom that he could sit beside her.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Statements and Tense Backshift</span>

### No backshift with present reporting verbs
If the reporting verb is present/present perfect/future, tense can stay the same.

Example:
- Direct: Paul says, "I am trying to get a taxi."
- Indirect: Paul says that he is trying to get a taxi.

### Usual backshift after past reporting verbs
| Direct Speech | Indirect Speech |
|---|---|
| Present Simple | Past Simple |
| Present Continuous | Past Continuous |
| Present Perfect | Past Perfect |
| Present Perfect Continuous | Past Perfect Continuous |
| Past Simple | Past Perfect |
| Past Continuous | Past Perfect Continuous |
| will/shall | would/should |
| can | could |
| may | might |

Examples:
- Direct: He said, "I never eat meat."
- Indirect: He explained that he never ate meat.
- Direct: He said, "I am waiting for Ann."
- Indirect: He said that he was waiting for Ann.
- Direct: He said, "I have found a flat."
- Indirect: He said that he had found a flat.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Questions in Reported Speech</span>
> [!info] Rule
> Reported questions use statement word order: subject + verb. No question mark.

### Yes/No questions
Use ask + if/whether + statement order.

Example:
- Direct: "Do you agree?" he asked.
- Indirect: He asked if I agreed.

### Wh-questions
Keep the wh-word, then use statement order.

Example:
- Direct: "Where are you going?" she asked.
- Indirect: She asked where I was going.

### Reporting verbs for questions
- If direct speech uses said, change to asked/wondered/wanted to know.
- Ask can take an object: He asked me...
- Wonder and want to know do not normally take an indirect object in this structure.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Commands, Requests, Advice</span>
Pattern:
- verb of command/request/advice + object + to-infinitive
- negative form: not + to-infinitive

Common verbs:
- advise, ask, beg, command, order, remind, tell, warn

Examples:
- Direct: "Close the door," she said.
- Indirect: She told me to close the door.
- Direct: "Do not be late," he said.
- Indirect: He warned us not to be late.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Exclamations, Greetings, Social Formulas</span>
Exclamations usually become statements in indirect speech.

Examples:
- Direct: "What a dreadful idea!"
- Indirect: He exclaimed that it was a dreadful idea.
- Direct: "Thank you."
- Indirect: He thanked me.
- Direct: "Good luck."
- Indirect: He wished me luck.
- Direct: "Congratulations."
- Indirect: He congratulated me.
- Direct: "Liar!"
- Indirect: He called me a liar.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Yes and No in Indirect Speech</span>
Yes/no responses are reported with a subject and suitable auxiliary.

Example:
- Direct: "Can you swim?" he asked. "No," I said.
- Indirect: He asked if I could swim, and I said that I could not.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Offers and Suggestions</span>

### Offers
- Direct: "Shall I bring you some tea?"
- Indirect: He offered to bring me some tea.

### Suggestions
- Direct: "Shall we meet at the theatre?"
- Indirect: He suggested meeting at the theatre.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Mixed Types in One Quote</span>
Long direct speech may mix statements, questions, and commands. Use separate reporting verbs.

Example:
- Direct: "I do not know the way. Do you?"
- Indirect: He said that he did not know the way and asked if I did.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Modals and Conditionals</span>

### Must
After a past reporting verb, must may stay as must in some contexts.

Example:
- Direct: He said, "It must be late."
- Indirect: He said that it must be late.

Had to is also common when meaning obligation.

### Past modals usually stay
Could, might, should, ought to, used to usually do not change.

Example:
- Direct: He said, "I might come."
- Indirect: He said that he might come.

### Type 2 conditionals often remain unchanged
Example:
- Direct: He said, "If my children were older, I would emigrate."
- Indirect: He said that if his children were older, he would emigrate.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Say vs Tell</span>

### Direct speech
- Say can introduce or follow direct quotes.
- Tell requires the person addressed.

Examples:
- "I have heard the news," Tom said.
- "I am leaving at once," Tom told me.

### Indirect speech
- Say + clause
- Tell + object + clause

Examples:
- He said (that) he had heard the news.
- He told me (that) he had heard the news.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Band 8.5 IELTS Upgrades</span>

### Use precise reporting verbs
Avoid overusing said/told.

Use:
- argue, assert, contend, claim, suggest, emphasize, highlight, warn, admit, deny

IELTS example:
- Many economists argue that taxes are too high.

### No-backshift exception (advanced control)
If the reported statement is still true, tense does not need backshift.

Examples:
- The scientist stated that water boils at 100 degrees.
- John mentioned that he lives in London. (still true now)

## <span style="color: #d4af37">High-Frequency Error Traps</span>
| Mistake | Incorrect | Correct | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| say vs tell | He told to me that he was tired. | He told me that he was tired. | Tell needs a direct object, not to-object. |
| question order | She asked what was my name. | She asked what my name was. | Reported questions use statement order. |
| double conjunction | He asked that if I was ready. | He asked if I was ready. | Do not combine that + if in reported questions. |
| suggest pattern | He suggested to go to the park. | He suggested going to the park. / He suggested that we go to the park. | Suggest is followed by gerund or that-clause. |

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Rapid Memory Sheet</span>
1. Report ideas, not quotation style, in IELTS.
2. Check pronoun -> tense -> time/place -> structure.
3. Questions lose question order and punctuation.
4. Commands become verb + object + to-infinitive.
5. Use stronger reporting verbs for Band 8+.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">IELTS-Focused Use</span>
- Writing Task 2: summarize views with formal reporting verbs.
- Speaking Part 3: report common opinion, then add your own stance.
- Editing pass: check pronouns, tense shifts, question order, and time markers.

> [!example] IELTS Example
> Environmental analysts contend that governments should invest in affordable public transport.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Practical Empowerment Flow for IELTS Aspirants</span>
Use this workflow in practice tests and real exam answers:

1. Capture the original idea, not the exact quote.
2. Choose a precise reporting verb: argue, claim, warn, suggest, emphasize.
3. Apply shifts fast: pronoun -> tense -> time/place.
4. Upgrade sentence shape: simple -> compound -> complex -> compound-complex.
5. Final check: statement order, no question mark in reported questions.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Sentence Variety Lab (High-Volume Transformations)</span>

### Simple Sentence Examples
| Direct Speech | Indirect Speech |
|---|---|
| He said, "I need clean water." | He said that he needed clean water. |
| The minister said, "We are improving roads." | The minister said that they were improving roads. |
| She said, "I have completed the survey." | She said that she had completed the survey. |
| They said, "We can reduce waste." | They said that they could reduce waste. |
| The teacher said, "I will check your essay." | The teacher said that she would check my essay. |
| He said, "This plan works." | He said that the plan worked. |
| She said, "I visited Dhaka yesterday." | She said that she had visited Dhaka the day before. |
| The analyst said, "Prices are rising now." | The analyst said that prices were rising then. |
| He said, "I may join the seminar." | He said that he might join the seminar. |
| She said, "My brother is here." | She said that her brother was there. |

### Compound Sentences with CC (FANBOYS)
| CC | Direct Speech | Indirect Speech |
|---|---|---|
| and | The mayor said, "We repaired roads, and we installed new lights." | The mayor said that they had repaired roads and had installed new lights. |
| but | The expert said, "The policy is expensive, but it is necessary." | The expert said that the policy was expensive but was necessary. |
| so | The officer said, "The buses were delayed, so commuters complained." | The officer said that the buses had been delayed, so commuters had complained. |
| yet | The report said, "Internet access improved, yet rural schools still struggled." | The report stated that internet access had improved, yet rural schools had still struggled. |
| or | The director said, "We can expand this year, or we can wait." | The director said that they could expand that year or could wait. |
| nor | The speaker said, "No one objected, nor did anyone leave early." | The speaker said that no one had objected, nor had anyone left early. |
| for | The economist said, "Taxes must rise, for public services are collapsing." | The economist said that taxes had to rise, for public services were collapsing. |

### Complex Sentence Examples
| Direct Speech | Indirect Speech |
|---|---|
| She said, "Although fuel costs are rising, people still drive daily." | She said that although fuel costs were rising, people still drove daily. |
| He said, "Because rents are high, young families are moving out." | He said that because rents were high, young families were moving out. |
| They said, "If the city builds more buses, traffic will decline." | They said that if the city built more buses, traffic would decline. |
| The doctor said, "When citizens exercise regularly, they stay healthier." | The doctor said that when citizens exercised regularly, they stayed healthier. |
| She said, "Unless the plan changes, we will fail." | She said that unless the plan changed, they would fail. |
| He said, "While we were discussing the budget, the power went out." | He said that while they had been discussing the budget, the power had gone out. |
| The teacher said, "After students submit this task, I will give feedback." | The teacher said that after students submitted that task, she would give feedback. |
| Analysts said, "If inflation falls, confidence may return." | Analysts said that if inflation fell, confidence might return. |
| He said, "Since I have studied hard, I feel ready." | He said that since he had studied hard, he felt ready. |
| She said, "Even though the exam is difficult, I am confident." | She said that even though the exam was difficult, she was confident. |

### Compound-Complex Sentence Examples
| Direct Speech | Indirect Speech |
|---|---|
| The minister said, "Although revenue fell, we protected schools, and we funded hospitals." | The minister said that although revenue had fallen, they had protected schools and had funded hospitals. |
| The mayor said, "If congestion worsens, we will expand rail, and we will restrict car use." | The mayor said that if congestion worsened, they would expand rail and would restrict car use. |
| She said, "Because my laptop crashed, I missed the deadline, but I emailed my tutor." | She said that because her laptop had crashed, she had missed the deadline, but she had emailed her tutor. |
| They said, "While we were waiting for approval, costs increased, so we revised the design." | They said that while they had been waiting for approval, costs had increased, so they had revised the design. |
| The examiner said, "If you organize your ideas clearly, your coherence will improve, and your score may rise." | The examiner said that if candidates organized their ideas clearly, their coherence would improve and their score might rise. |
| He said, "Although the village is remote, people adapt quickly, and they support one another." | He said that although the village was remote, people adapted quickly and supported one another. |
| The report said, "Because exports dropped, firms cut jobs, and the government launched support schemes." | The report said that because exports had dropped, firms had cut jobs, and the government had launched support schemes. |
| She said, "If I finish this draft tonight, I will submit it tomorrow, and I will start the next task." | She said that if she finished that draft that night, she would submit it the next day and would start the next task. |

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Practical Implication by IELTS Task</span>

### Writing Task 2: Argument Flow
Use this sequence in body paragraphs:
1. Topic claim with reporting verb.
2. Supporting reason in a complex sentence.
3. Contrast with a compound sentence using but or yet.
4. Final consequence with therefore/consequently.

Model flow:
- Many policy analysts argue that rent control protects low-income families.
- They add that when rents rise rapidly, vulnerable groups are displaced.
- Critics acknowledge this point, but they warn that strict controls may reduce housing supply.
- Consequently, governments need balanced regulation.

### Speaking Part 3: High-Score Response Flow
1. Report a social view indirectly.
2. Add your qualified opinion.
3. Give one real example.

Model response:
- Many parents claim that screen time harms concentration.
- I partly agree, although I think educational content can still be useful.
- For example, several students in my area said that structured learning apps had helped them revise consistently.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">CC Connector Power Drills (Quick Conversion)</span>
Convert each direct sentence to indirect speech:
1. "The plan is costly, but it is effective," she said.
2. "Fuel prices fell, so transport became cheaper," he said.
3. "The policy seems simple, yet implementation is difficult," they said.
4. "We can reduce waste, or we can keep ignoring the problem," the mayor said.
5. "No one complained, nor did anyone leave," the host said.

Answer key:
1. She said that the plan was costly but effective.
2. He said that fuel prices had fallen, so transport had become cheaper.
3. They said that the policy seemed simple, yet implementation was difficult.
4. The mayor said that they could reduce waste or could keep ignoring the problem.
5. The host said that no one had complained, nor had anyone left.

3. Did I update time/place markers?
4. Did I keep statement order in reported questions?
5. Did I use at least one complex and one compound structure?
6. Did I use one precise reporting verb beyond said/told?
