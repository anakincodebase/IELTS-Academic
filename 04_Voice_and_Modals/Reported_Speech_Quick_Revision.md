# Reported (Indirect) Speech (Part 1 - Statements)

**Source:** Anglo-Link | English Grammar Lesson

## 1. Direct vs. Indirect Speech
When we want to repeat what someone else has said, we switch from direct speech to indirect (reported) speech.
* **Direct Speech:** John said, "I often come here."
* **Indirect Speech:** John said (that) he often came here.

### What changes?
1.  **Pronouns:** Change to indicate the person who originally spoke (e.g., *I* changes to *he*).
2.  **Verb Tenses:** Shift backwards if the reporting verb is in the past tense (e.g., *come* changes to *came*).
3.  **Time and Place Markers:** Adjust to reflect the new time or perspective.

*Note:* You can connect the reporting verb and the reported statement with or without the word "**that**".

## 2. Reporting Verbs
The most common reporting verbs are **say**, **tell**, and **explain**.
* **Present Tense Reporting Verbs:** If the reporting verb is in the present (e.g., *says*, *is saying*), there is **no tense change** in the reported statement.
* **Say & Explain:** If you specify the person being spoken to, you must use the preposition "**to**" (e.g., *He said to her*, *They explained to us*).
* **Tell:** You should specify the person directly **without** "to" (e.g., *He told her*, *They told us*).

## 3. Tense Changes
When the reporting verb is in the past tense, you must shift the verbs in the reported speech back a tense:

**To Be & Modal Verbs:**
* am / is $\rightarrow$ was
* are $\rightarrow$ were
* was / were $\rightarrow$ had been
* can $\rightarrow$ could
* must $\rightarrow$ must / had to
* may $\rightarrow$ might
* might $\rightarrow$ might *(no change)*
* could $\rightarrow$ could / had been able to
* shall $\rightarrow$ should
* should / ought to $\rightarrow$ *(no change)*

**Simple & Perfect Tenses:**
* do / does (Present Simple) $\rightarrow$ did (Past Simple)
* did (Past Simple) $\rightarrow$ had done (Past Perfect)
* will do $\rightarrow$ would do
* have / has done (Present Perfect) $\rightarrow$ had done (Past Perfect)
* had done (Past Perfect) $\rightarrow$ had done *(no change)*
* will have done $\rightarrow$ would have done

**Continuous Tenses:**
* am / is / are doing $\rightarrow$ was / were doing
* have / has been doing $\rightarrow$ had been doing
* was / were doing $\rightarrow$ had been doing
* had been doing $\rightarrow$ had been doing *(no change)*
* will be doing $\rightarrow$ would be doing
* will have been doing $\rightarrow$ would have been doing

**Other Verb Forms:**
* used to $\rightarrow$ used to *(no change)*
* am / is / are going to $\rightarrow$ was / were going to

## 4. Time and Place Markers
To keep the sense of the sentence logical, time and location words must shift:
* **tomorrow** $\rightarrow$ the next day / the following day
* **yesterday** $\rightarrow$ the day before
* **next** week/month/year $\rightarrow$ the **following** week/month/year
* **last** year/week/night $\rightarrow$ the year/week/night **before**
* **now** $\rightarrow$ then / immediately

## 5. Other Important Word Changes
* **this / these** $\rightarrow$ **that / those** *(e.g., "We've chosen these books" $\rightarrow$ They told us they'd chosen those books.)*
* **here** $\rightarrow$ **there** *(if the speaker's location has changed since the original statement)*
* **"would like"** $\rightarrow$ **"want"**. Since "I'd like to" is a formal equivalent of "I want" used in the present tense, it shifts to "wanted" in reported speech. 
    *(e.g., "I'd like to be a singer" $\rightarrow$ She said she wanted to be a singer.)

# Indirect & Reported Questions (Part 2)



## 1. Reporting Verbs for Questions
* The most common reporting verb for questions is **ask**.
* When using "ask", you have the choice to specify the person you are speaking to or not.
* **Crucial Rule:** If you specify the person, **do not use the preposition "to"**. 
  * *Correct:* He asked me. / He asked Jane.
  * *Incorrect:* He asked to me. / He asked to him.

## 2. Changing Questions back to Statements
Other than the standard shifts in pronouns, tenses, and time markers, there is one major rule for indirect questions: **You must change the question form back into a normal statement form (Subject + Verb order).**
* *Direct:* He asked me, "When **can I** call you back?"
* *Indirect:* He asked me when **he could** call me back. *(Notice the order is NOT "when could he")*
* *Direct:* They asked us, "Where **will you** be?"
* *Indirect:* They asked us where **we would** be.

## 3. Yes/No Questions (Closed Questions)
When reporting a closed question (a question that can be answered with a simple "yes" or "no"), you need to connect the reporting verb and the reported question using the word "**if**".
* *Direct:* We asked Andrew, "Have you bought a new game?"
* *Indirect:* We asked Andrew **if** he had bought a new game.
* *Direct:* They asked us, "Are you French?"
* *Indirect:* They asked us **if** we were French.

## 4. Polite or Formal Questions
Indirect question structures are frequently used to sound more polite or formal. Common introductory expressions include:
* *Could you tell me...*
* *Would you mind telling me...*
* *May I ask...*
* *I was wondering...*

**Examples:**
* *Direct:* Where did you go? $\rightarrow$ *Indirect:* Could you tell me where you went?
* *Direct:* How old are you? $\rightarrow$ *Indirect:* May I ask how old you are?

*Note on "I was wondering":* Because this introductory phrase is in the past tense, you must apply the standard tense shift backwards to the original sentence.
* *Direct:* How much did you pay? $\rightarrow$ *Indirect:* I was wondering how much you **had paid**.

## 5. Specific Cases (Functions)
When reporting sentences that serve a specific conversational function (like inviting, ordering, or apologizing), it is much more natural and concise to use a specific reporting verb rather than simply "asked" or "said".

* **Requests (Ask):**
  * *Direct:* "Slow down, please." $\rightarrow$ *Indirect:* He **asked** me to slow down.
* **Orders (Tell):**
  * *Direct:* "Stop shouting." $\rightarrow$ *Indirect:* He **told** him to stop shouting.
* **Suggestions / Recommendations (Suggest / Recommend):**
  * *Direct:* "Why don't we write a letter?" $\rightarrow$ *Indirect:* She **suggested** writing a letter.
* **Invitations (Invite):**
  * *Direct:* "Would you like to dance?" $\rightarrow$ *Indirect:* I **invited** her to dance. *(More natural and shorter than "I asked her if she wanted to dance.")*
* **Offers (Offer):**
  * *Direct:* "Shall I drive you home?" $\rightarrow$ *Indirect:* She **offered** to drive them home.
* **Apologies (Apologize):**
  * *Direct:* "Sorry for the inconvenience." $\rightarrow$ *Indirect:* We **apologized** to him for the inconvenience.
