# Linking Words of Purpose

**Source:** Anglo-Link | English Lesson: In order to, With a view to, So that

## Introduction
Linking words of purpose connect an action (in the main clause) to its desired result (in the subordinate clause). They answer the question "Why are you doing this?"
* *Action A (Main Clause)* $\rightarrow$ *Action B (Subordinate Clause)*

---

## 1. Categories of Linking Words of Purpose

### A. Informal
Used in everyday, casual language.
* **to** + base verb
* **so** + new sentence

*Rules for Informal:*
* **Same Subject:** If the same person performs both actions, you can use either.
    * *Example:* I've moved out the furniture **to** paint the room.
    * *Example:* I've moved out the furniture **so** I can paint the room.
* **Different Subjects:** If different people perform the actions, you *must* use **so**.
    * *Example:* I've moved out the furniture **so** we can paint the room.

### B. Neutral
Standard phrasing used in both spoken and written English.
* **in order to** + base verb
* **so that** + new sentence

*Rules for Neutral:*
* **Same Subject:** Both can be used.
    * *Example:* I've moved out the furniture **in order to** paint the room.
    * *Example:* I've moved out the furniture **so that** I can paint the room.
* **Different Subjects:** You *must* use **so that**.
    * *Example:* I've moved out the furniture **so that** we can paint the room.

### C. Formal
Used in professional, academic, or formal writing.

**Subcategory 1: Linking Expressions**
* **so as to** + base verb
* **in order that** + new sentence

*Rules for Linking Expressions:*
* **Same Subject:** Both can be used.
    * *Example:* They amended the law **so as to** protect employees.
    * *Example:* They amended the law **in order that** they could protect employees.
* **Different Subjects:** You *must* use **in order that**.
    * *Example:* They amended the law **in order that** it could protect employees.

**Subcategory 2: Compound Prepositions**
These are only appropriate when the subject is the same in both clauses. Crucially, they must be followed by a **gerund** (verb + -ing).
* **for the purpose of** + -ing
* **with the aim of** + -ing
* **with the intention of** + -ing
* **with a view to** + -ing

*Example:* They amended the law **with the aim of protecting** employees' rights.

---

## 2. Important Notes for Usage

**Note 1: Emphasizing Purpose**
If you want to emphasize the purpose, start the sentence with the subordinate clause. When writing, use a comma. When speaking, stress the clause with your voice.
* *Example:* **To avoid any further delays,** let's make a decision now.
* *Example:* **With a view to accelerating the process,** we've eliminated phase two.

**Note 2: Modal Verbs in Subordinate Clauses**
Clauses starting with *so, so that,* and *in order that* usually require a modal verb.
* **Present/Future (Positive):** Use **can** (e.g., ...so I can get a tan).
* **Present/Future (Negative):** Use **won't** (e.g., ...so I won't get sunburned).
* **Past (Positive):** Use **could** (e.g., ...so I could get a tan).
* **Past (Negative):** Use **wouldn't** (e.g., ...so I wouldn't get sunburned).

---

## 3. Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Mistake 1: Using "for" + Gerund to express purpose**
* *Incorrect:* I am sitting in the sun for getting a suntan.
* *Correct:* I am sitting in the sun **to get / in order to get** a suntan.
* *Exception:* You CAN use "for" if it is followed by a *real noun*, not a gerund (e.g., "He is in Berlin **for the exhibition**").

**Mistake 2: Incorrect Negative Subordinate Clauses**
Do not say "to don't" or "to not". When the verb is negative, you *must* use **in order not to** or **so as not to**.
* *Incorrect:* I'm sitting in the shade to don't get sunburned.
* *Correct:* I'm sitting in the shade **in order not to / so as not to** get sunburned.

*Note on Negative Modals:* As mentioned in Note 2, if you use a "so/so that" structure in the negative, use *won't* or *wouldn't*, not *can't* or *couldn't*.
* *Incorrect:* I sat in the shade so I couldn't get sunburned.
* *Correct:* I sat in the shade so I **wouldn't** get sunburned.

**Mistake 3: Forgetting the Gerund after Compound Prepositions**
Because words like "to" (in *with a view to*) and "of" (in *with the intention of*) function as prepositions here, they must be followed by the -ing form, not the base verb.
* *Incorrect:* With a view to protect...
* *Correct:* With a view **to protecting**...
* *Incorrect:* With the intention of increase...
* *Correct:* With the intention **of increasing**...
