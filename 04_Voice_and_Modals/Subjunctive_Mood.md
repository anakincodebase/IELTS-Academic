# The Subjunctive Mood

**Source:** Anglo-Link | English Grammar Lesson

## Introduction to the Subjunctive
The subjunctive mood is a specific verb form used to express:
* That something is important or recommended.
* A wish or a desire.
* An unreal, improbable, or hypothetical condition.

*Note on Regional Differences:* The present subjunctive is very common in American English. In British English, the present subjunctive is often replaced by an alternative structure, but the past subjunctive remains common (especially in conditionals and expressions like "I wish" or "if only").

---

## 1. The Present Subjunctive

The present subjunctive is primarily used in formal English after verbs and expressions that indicate recommendation, importance, or demand.

**Form:** It has the **exact same form as the base verb** (the infinitive without "to"). This form does not change for the third-person singular (no 's') and remains the same regardless of whether the main situation is in the present or the past.

**Trigger Expressions (Importance):**
* It is important...
* It is essential...
* It is vital...
* It is crucial...

**Trigger Verbs (Recommendations/Demands):**
* Suggest
* Recommend
* Propose
* Insist
* Demand

**Examples:**
* *Present context:* It is necessary that they **be** warned of the risks.
* *Past context:* It was vital that they **be** warned of the risks.
* *Using a verb:* I recommend that he **see** a specialist. (Not *sees*)
* *Past context:* I recommended that he **see** a specialist.

**Negative Subjunctive:**
Negative present subjunctive constructions are rare even in formal English. It is better to use a direct antonym. 
* *Instead of:* "It is important that she not be late."
* *Use:* "It is important that she be punctual."

### Alternatives to the Present Subjunctive
In British English (and informal spoken English generally), alternatives are often preferred:
1. **Using the modal "should" (British English preference):**
   * *Example:* It is necessary that they **should be** warned of the risks.
   * *Example:* I recommend that he **should see** a specialist.
2. **Using "for" + infinitive with "to":**
   * *Example:* It is necessary **for them to be** warned of the risks.
3. **Using normal tenses (Informal/Everyday English):**
   * *Formal:* It is necessary that they be warned...
   * *Informal:* It's necessary they **are warned**... (often dropping "that")
   * *Formal:* I recommended that he delay seeing...
   * *Informal:* I recommended he **delayed** seeing...

---

## 2. The Past Subjunctive

The past subjunctive is used to talk about **unreal, hypothetical, or improbable present or future situations**.

**Form:**
It has the exact same form as the **past simple tense**, with one major exception: for the verb "to be," the past subjunctive form is **"were" for all persons** (including *I, he, she, it*). 
* *(Note: In informal English, "was" is sometimes used for the 1st and 3rd person singular, but "were" is highly recommended to be grammatically safe).*

**Common Usage:**
**1. Conditional Type 2 (If Clauses):**
* Used for hypothetical situations.
* *Example:* If I **were** young, I would travel around the world. (Not *was*)
* *Example:* If I **didn't have** to do this, I would catch up on my reading.

**2. Other Expressions for Unreal Situations:**
* **It's time:** *It's time the Prime Minister **took** the matter in hand.*
* **I wish:** *I wish it **weren't** so gray today.*
* **If only:** *If only I **had** a faster computer.*
* **I'd rather:** *I'd rather you **didn't tell** everyone.*
* **Look / Behave / Sound + as if / as though:** *She behaves as if she **were** the last woman on earth.*

**Special Notes for Past Subjunctive Exceptions:**
* **"I wish" + "would":** If you want to indicate that you are annoyed by a current situation and want it to change, you can use "would" after "I wish." 
  * *Example:* I wish you **would stop** shouting at the TV.
* **Real/Probable Situations with "as if":** If a situation looks real or highly probable, you use normal present tenses rather than the subjunctive.
  * *Example:* She looks as if she **has heard** some great news. (Because it genuinely appears true).2


# The Subjunctive in Spoken English

**Source:** Anglo-Link | English Grammar Lesson 

## Introduction
The subjunctive is a **form** (not a tense) used after certain verbs and expressions. It is often called the "subjunctive mood." 
* **Present Subjunctive:** Very rarely used in spoken English (especially British English).
* **Past Subjunctive:** Frequently used in specific cases in spoken English.

---

## 1. Formulation of the Subjunctive

**Present Subjunctive Formulation:**
* It has the exact same form as the **base verb** (the infinitive without "to").
* *Example:* It is essential that he **be** transparent.

**Past Subjunctive Formulation:**
* It has the same form as the **past simple**.
* *Crucial Exception:* For the verb "to be", the past subjunctive has only ONE form for all subjects: **were**. (e.g., *If I were, if he were, if they were*).

---

## 2. The Present Subjunctive

Used primarily in formal situations. Even though it's rare in spoken English, you must understand it to recognize it. The situation described can be in the present or past, but the form remains the same.

**A. After Expressions of Importance:**
* *It is important, essential, vital, crucial, necessary, of vital importance.*
* *Formal Example:* It is essential that the president **be** transparent.

**B. After Certain Verbs:**
* *Suggest, recommend, propose, insist, demand, ask, command, order.*
* *Formal Example:* The opposition leader demanded that she **clarify** her position. (Not *clarified* or *clarifies*).

### How to Avoid the Present Subjunctive in Informal Speech:
Because these sound very formal, native speakers avoid them in three ways:

1.  **Using "for someone to do" (with expressions of importance):**
    * *Instead of:* It is important that she be honest.
    * *Say:* It is important **for her to be** honest.
2.  **Using the modal verb "should" (with specific verbs):**
    * *Instead of:* I hope you insisted that she be honest.
    * *Say:* I hope you insisted that she **should be** honest.
3.  **Ignoring the rule and using normal tenses (grammatically incorrect but accepted):**
    * *Instead of:* It is vital that she be honest.
    * *Say:* It's vital she **is** honest.
    * *Instead of:* I insisted she be honest.
    * *Say:* I insisted she **was** honest.

*Note on Negative Present Subjunctive:* It sounds very awkward (e.g., "It is crucial that he **not withhold** information"). To avoid this, use "should not" or change to a positive verb ("It is crucial that he **share** all information").

---

## 3. The Past Subjunctive

The past subjunctive is essential in spoken English for certain expressions. Although it looks like the past simple, it usually describes a hypothetical **present or future** situation.

**Common Expressions Requiring Past Subjunctive:**
1.  **It is time:** *It is time he **started** saving up.*
2.  **I would rather:** *I'd rather you **didn't** call me that.*
3.  **As if / As though:** *He behaves as if he **owned** this place.*
4.  **I wish:** *I wish I **were** 10 years younger.*
5.  **If only:** *If only she **knew** how to sing.*
6.  **Second Conditional (If Clauses):** *If he **had** a choice, he would live elsewhere.*

### Avoiding the Past Subjunctive (Where Possible)
You can only find alternatives for the first two expressions:
* *Instead of:* It is time he started saving... $\rightarrow$ *Say:* It is time **for him to start** saving...
* *Instead of:* I'd rather you didn't... $\rightarrow$ *Say:* I would **prefer you not to**...

### Where You CANNOT Avoid the Past Subjunctive:
You **must** use the past subjunctive in the following cases to express hypothetical or unreal ideas:
1.  **As if / As though (Unreal Situations):** * *Unreal/Hypothetical:* He behaves as if he **owned** this place. (We know he doesn't).
    * *(Note: If you use a normal present tense like "He behaves as though he owns this place," it implies a probable situation—maybe he really does own it).*
2.  **I wish / If only:**
    * *Example:* I wish I **were** 10 years younger.
3.  **Second Conditional (If Clauses):**
    * *Example:* If he **had** a choice...

*Final Notes on Past Subjunctive:*
* Always use "**were**" instead of "was" for formal/correct English (e.g., *If I were*, not *If I was*).
* If you are expressing regret about a situation that actually happened in the past (after "I wish" or "if only"), you must use the **Past Perfect tense**.
    * *Example:* I wish I **had started** saving up last year.
