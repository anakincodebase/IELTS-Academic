# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Active and Passive Voice</span>

Active voice focuses on the doer. Passive voice focuses on the action or result.

Most English sentences are active: "The government implemented the policy." But passive voice serves specific purposes: "The policy was implemented by the government" or more commonly in formal writing, "The policy was implemented" (leaving out the doer).

IELTS doesn't demand passive voice, but it appears naturally in academic writing, especially in Task 1. Understanding when to use each and why separates Band 6 from Band 8.

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Structure: Active vs. Passive</span>

**Active voice:** Subject performs the action.  
Formula: Subject + Verb + Object  
"The committee approved the plan."

**Passive voice:** Subject receives the action.  
Formula: Object + be + past participle (+ by + agent)  
"The plan was approved by the committee."  
Or without stating who did it:  
"The plan was approved."

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">When to Use Each</span>

Use **active voice** when the doer is important or when you want directness and clarity:  
"The manager revised the policy because the previous system had failed."  
"Governments must invest in public transport." (The doer—governments—is what matters.)

Use **passive voice** when the action is more important than the doer, when the doer is unknown, or when you want formal tone:  
"The new policy was implemented company-wide." (We don't need to say who did it.)

## <span style="color: #00838f">Structure: Active vs Passive</span>

### <span style="color: #00838f">What Is Active Voice?</span>
In active voice, the subject performs the action.

- Active structure: Subject + Verb + Object
- Example: The cat chased the mouse.

### <span style="color: #00838f">What Is Passive Voice?</span>
In passive voice, the subject receives the action.

- Passive structure: Object + form of be + V3 (+ by + agent)
- Example: The mouse was chased by the cat.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Sentence Variety Examples</span>

### <span style="color: #00838f">Active Voice Variety</span>
- Simple: The committee approved the plan.
- Compound: The committee approved the plan, and the finance team released the budget.
- Complex: Because the committee approved the plan, the finance team released the budget.
- Compound-Complex: Although the committee requested revisions, it approved the plan, and the finance team released the budget the same day.

### <span style="color: #00838f">Passive Voice Variety</span>
- Simple: The plan was approved by the committee.
- Compound: The plan was approved by the committee, and the budget was released by the finance team.
- Complex: Because the plan was approved by the committee, the budget was released by the finance team.
- Compound-Complex: Although revisions were requested by the committee, the plan was approved, and the budget was released the same day.

## When to Use Each

### <span style="color: #5e35b1">Use Active Voice</span>
- when you want direct, clear, concise sentences
- when the doer is important
- for engaging storytelling, persuasion, and instructions

Examples:
- The teacher explained the lesson clearly, and the students applied the method in class.
- Although the streets were crowded, the hero saved the town before sunrise.
- The manager revised the policy because the previous system had failed.

### <span style="color: #5e35b1">Use Passive Voice</span>
- when the action/result is more important than the doer
- when the doer is unknown, obvious, or irrelevant
- for formal and academic tone

Examples:
- The new policy was implemented by the company, and employee training was completed within two weeks.
- The cookies were eaten before the guests arrived, so a second batch was baked.
- The experiment was conducted by the researchers after the equipment had been calibrated.
- When the final report was released, several errors were identified and corrected.

> [!example] IELTS Example
> Task 1 process language: Plastic bottles are collected, cleaned, and recycled.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">How to Form Active and Passive Sentences</span>

### <span style="color: #00838f">Active Voice Structure</span>
1. Subject + Verb + Object
   - Example: The chef cooked the meal.

### <span style="color: #00838f">Passive Voice Structure</span>
1. Object + form of be + V3 (+ by + Subject)
   - Example: The meal was cooked by the chef.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Active Voice: Tense, Structure, and Example</span>

| Tense | Active Structure | Active Example |
|---|---|---|
| Simple Present | Subject + V1/V1+s + Object | I make a cake. |
| Present Continuous | Subject + am/is/are + V-ing + Object | I am making a cake. |
| Present Perfect | Subject + has/have + V3 + Object | I have made a cake. |
| Present Perfect Continuous | Subject + has/have + been + V-ing + Object | I have been making a cake. |
| Simple Past | Subject + V2 + Object | I made a cake yesterday. |
| Past Continuous | Subject + was/were + V-ing + Object | I was making a cake. |
| Past Perfect | Subject + had + V3 + Object | I had made a cake. |
| Past Perfect Continuous | Subject + had + been + V-ing + Object | I had been making a cake. |
| Future Simple | Subject + will/shall + V1 + Object | I will make a cake. |
| Future Continuous | Subject + will/shall + be + V-ing + Object | I will be making a cake. |
| Future Perfect | Subject + will/shall + have + V3 + Object | I will have made a cake. |
| Modal | Subject + modal + V1 + Object | I can make a cake. |

## <span style="color: #00838f">Passive Voice: Tense, Structure, and Example</span>

| Tense | Passive Structure | Passive Example |
|---|---|---|
| Simple Present | Subject + am/is/are + V3 (+ by) | A cake is made by me. |
| Present Continuous | Subject + am/is/are + being + V3 (+ by) | A cake is being made by me. |
| Present Perfect | Subject + has/have + been + V3 (+ by) | A cake has been made by me. |
| Present Perfect Continuous | Subject + has/have + been + being + V3 (+ by) | A cake has been being made by me. (rare) |
| Simple Past | Subject + was/were + V3 (+ by) | A cake was made by me yesterday. |
| Past Continuous | Subject + was/were + being + V3 (+ by) | A cake was being made by me. |
| Past Perfect | Subject + had + been + V3 (+ by) | A cake had been made by me. |
| Past Perfect Continuous | Subject + had + been + being + V3 (+ by) | A cake had been being made by me. (rare) |
| Future Simple | Subject + will/shall + be + V3 (+ by) | A cake will be made by me. |
| Future Continuous | No standard passive form in natural usage | - |
| Future Perfect | Subject + will/shall + have + been + V3 (+ by) | A cake will have been made by me. |
| Modal | Subject + modal + be + V3 (+ by) | A cake can be made by me. |

> [!info] Note
> Present Perfect Continuous and Past Perfect Continuous have passive forms in theory (has/have/had been being + V3), but they are uncommon and usually avoided in natural IELTS writing and speaking.

> [!info] Note
> Future Continuous generally has no natural passive form in standard usage.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Tense Conversion Formulas</span>

- Simple Present: V1/V1+s -> am/is/are + V3
  - Active: The mason builds the wall.
  - Passive: The wall is built by the mason.
- Present Continuous: am/is/are + V-ing -> am/is/are + being + V3
  - Active: Some boys are helping the wounded man.
  - Passive: The wounded man is being helped by some boys.
- Present Perfect: has/have + V3 -> has/have + been + V3
- Present Perfect Continuous: has/have + been + V-ing -> has/have + been + being + V3 (rare)
- Simple Past: V2 -> was/were + V3
  - Active: Brutus stabbed Caesar.
  - Passive: Caesar was stabbed by Brutus.
- Past Continuous: was/were + V-ing -> was/were + being + V3
- Past Perfect: had + V3 -> had + been + V3
- Past Perfect Continuous: had + been + V-ing -> had + been + being + V3 (rare)
- Simple Future: will/shall + V1 -> will/shall + be + V3
  - Active: He will finish the work in a fortnight.
  - Passive: The work will be finished by him in a fortnight.
- Future Continuous: will/shall + be + V-ing -> no standard passive form in natural usage
- Future Perfect: will/shall + have + V3 -> will/shall + have been + V3
- Modals: can/may/must + V1 -> can/may/must + be + V3
- Modal Perfect: modal + have + V3 -> modal + have been + V3

## <span style="color: #00838f">Advanced Passive Structures</span>

### <span style="color: #00838f">Verbs with Two Objects</span>
Both passive forms are possible.

- Active: Mr. Krishnaji teaches us grammar.
- Passive 1 (person focus): We are taught grammar by Mr. Krishnaji.
- Passive 2 (thing focus): Grammar is taught to us by Mr. Krishnaji.

### <span style="color: #00838f">Interrogative Sentences</span>
The sentence must remain a question after transformation.

- Active: Who taught you French?
- Passive: By whom was French taught to you? / Who were you taught French by?

- Active: Why did your brother write such a letter?
- Passive: Why was such a letter written by your brother?

### <span style="color: #00838f">Infinitive Constructions</span>

- Active: It is time to shut up the shop.
- Passive: It is time for the shop to be shut up.

### <span style="color: #00838f">Imperative Sentences</span>
- Active: Close the door.
- Passive: Let the door be closed.

- Active: Finish the report by noon.
- Passive: Let the report be finished by noon.

### E) Reporting Verb Passives
- Active: People say that the company is expanding rapidly.
- Passive 1: It is said that the company is expanding rapidly.
- Passive 2: The company is said to be expanding rapidly.

### F) Causative Passive (Have/Get + Object + V3)
- I had my laptop repaired because the screen was flickering.
- She got her passport renewed before she applied for the visa.

### G) Gerund and Infinitive Passives
- Passive infinitive: The files need to be reviewed before the meeting starts.
- Passive gerund: He dislikes being interrupted while he is presenting.

### H) Prepositional Verb Passives
- Active: They laughed at the idea, but they later adopted it.
- Passive: The idea was laughed at at first, but it was later adopted.

## Usage Rules: When to Use Passive

### Omit the Agent (by-phrase) when:
1. the agent is unknown, vague, or indefinite
   - Active: Somebody has stolen my pen.
   - Passive: My pen has been stolen.
2. the agent is obvious from context
   - Active: The audience loudly cheered the mayor's speech.
   - Passive: The mayor's speech was loudly cheered.

### Instruments vs Agents
- Use by for the living doer (agent).
  - The dog was hit by a boy.
- Use with for the instrument.
  - The dog was hit with a stick.

## Comprehensive Grammar Rules Checklist

### Core Formation Rules
- Only transitive verbs (verbs that take objects) can normally be passivized.
- In passive transformation, the object of the active sentence becomes the subject of the passive sentence.
- Keep the same tense and aspect when converting active to passive.
- Use the correct form of be according to tense, subject number, and person.
- Always use V3 (past participle) in passive constructions.

### Agreement and Word Order Rules
- Make the auxiliary agree with the new subject in the passive sentence.
- In questions, invert the first auxiliary and the subject.
- In negatives, place not after the first auxiliary.
- Place adverbs naturally: The proposal was quickly approved by the board.
- Avoid awkward word order with repeated by-phrases.

### Agent Rules
- Add by + agent only when the doer is important or unclear without it.
- Omit the agent when it is unknown, obvious, or unimportant.
- Use with (not by) for instruments and tools.

### Tense and Aspect Rules
- Avoid overusing rare passive continuous-perfect forms in natural speaking/writing.
- Prefer clear alternatives when a formal passive sounds unnatural.
- Future continuous usually has no natural passive equivalent.
- Modal passive: modal + be + V3.
- Modal perfect passive: modal + have been + V3.

### Special Structure Rules
- Two-object verbs can form two valid passive versions.
- Wh-questions keep the Wh-word and change only the auxiliary-verb structure.
- Imperatives in passive commonly use let + object + be + V3.
- Reporting verb passives are common in formal writing: It is believed that..., X is believed to...
- Gerund and infinitive passives are common after certain verbs and adjectives.

### Style Rules (IELTS Focus)
- Use active voice for clarity and energy in Speaking and argument sentences.
- Use passive voice for process descriptions and objective tone in Task 1 and Task 2.
- Mix both voices strategically to improve grammatical range and coherence.
- Do not force passive voice into every sentence; choose based on focus.
- Keep sentence control first, then add complexity with subordinate clauses.

## Exceptions: Active Form, Passive Sense
Some verbs can appear in active form but carry passive meaning.

- These mangoes taste sour.
- The rose smells sweet.
- The play reads well.
- This fabric washes easily.
- The book sells quickly when the review is positive.

## Interrogative Conversion Formulas

### Yes/No Questions
- Active (Do/Does): Do/Does + Subject + V1 + Object?
- Passive: Is/Am/Are + Object + V3 + by + Subject?

- Active (Did): Did + Subject + V1 + Object?
- Passive: Was/Were + Object + V3 + by + Subject?

- Active (continuous): Is/Am/Are/Was/Were + Subject + V-ing + Object?
- Passive: Is/Am/Are/Was/Were + Object + being + V3 + by + Subject?

### Wh- Questions
- Keep the Wh-word unchanged.
- Then convert the remaining structure using passive question order.

Examples:
- Active: Why did they delay the meeting?
- Passive: Why was the meeting delayed?

- Active: When did the committee announce the result?
- Passive: When was the result announced by the committee?

- Active: Who approved the final budget?
- Passive: By whom was the final budget approved? / Who was the final budget approved by?

## High-Value IELTS Uses
- Task 1 process/map: use passive heavily for steps, stages, and systems.
- Task 2: use passive for objective tone (It is widely believed that...).
- Speaking: use active for natural fluency; add passive selectively for formal ideas.
