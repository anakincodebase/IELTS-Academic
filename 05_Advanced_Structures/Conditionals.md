# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Conditionals</span>

Conditionals express "if this happens, then that happens." English has four main types, ranging from universal facts (if you heat ice, it melts) to imaginary past situations (if I had studied harder, I would have passed).

Mastering conditionals signals advanced grammar control. They appear naturally in Task 2 (especially in arguments about consequences) and in Speaking Part 3 (discussing hypothetical situations).

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Conditional 0: Universal Facts</span>

**Use for:** Things that are always true, scientific facts, universal rules.

**Form:** If + present simple, present simple

"If demand for a product rises, its price rises."  
"If you heat ice, it melts."

You can replace "if" with "when" without changing meaning: "When you heat ice, it melts."

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Conditional 1: Real Future</span>

**Use for:** Realistic situations that are likely to happen.

**Form:** If + present simple, will + base verb

"If the government invests in public transport, congestion will decrease."  
"If you study consistently, you will improve your writing."

You can also use may/might for less certainty: "If policymakers act, emissions may stabilize."

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Conditional 2: Hypothetical Present/Future</span>

**Use for:** Imaginary situations, low probability, things contrary to reality.

**Form:** If + past simple, would + base verb

"If I had more time, I would study more carefully." (But I don't have time.)  
"If governments prioritized prevention, health systems would be more efficient." (They often don't.)

**Important:** With "to be," always use "were": "If I were a policymaker..." (Not "If I was...")

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Structure 1: Conditional 0 (The Zero Conditional)</span>

* **Concept:** Used to express universal facts or things that are always true. It does not describe a hypothetical situation.
* **Formulation:** `If + Present Simple`, `Present Simple`
* **Examples:**
  * *If* you **heat** ice, it **melts**.
  * *If* demand for a product **rises**, its price **rises**.
* **Linguistic Nuance:** Because the zero conditional deals with absolute certainties, you can seamlessly replace **If** with **When** without altering the meaning. (e.g., *When you heat ice, it melts*).

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Structure 2: Conditional 1 (The First Conditional)</span>

* **Concept:** Used for a present or future situation of **high probability**. It describes realistic, likely outcomes based on a specific condition.
* **Formulation:** `If + Present Simple`, `Future Simple (will + base verb)`
* **Examples:**
  * *If* I **see** her tomorrow, I **will speak** to her.
  * *If* they **are** late again, I **will not let** them in.
* **Special Cases:**
  * **Imperatives:** You can use an imperative in the consequence clause. (e.g., *If you **see** John, please **give** him this book*).
  * **Politeness:** You may occasionally see *would* inside the 'if clause' strictly as a marker of formal politeness. (e.g., *If you **would follow** me, I will show you your room*).

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Structure 3: Conditional 2 (The Second Conditional)</span>

* **Concept:** Used for a present or future situation of **low probability or zero probability**. It describes hypothetical, imaginary, or unreal situations.
* **Formulation:** `If + Past Simple`, `would + base verb`
* **Examples (Low Probability):**
  * I don't think it will change, but *if* anything **changed**, we **would let** you know.
* **Examples (Zero Probability / Hypothetical):**
  * *If* I **had** some time, I **would tidy** up my office.
* **Crucial Rule (The Subjunctive "Were"):** In the second conditional, the verb 'to be' becomes **were** for *all* pronouns (I, he, she, it). While "was" is sometimes heard in highly informal speech, **were** is strictly required for correct, elevated British English.
  * *Correct:* If I **were** a lawyer, I wouldn't take this case. (Not *If I was...*)

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Structure 4: Conditional 3 (The Third Conditional)</span>

* **Concept:** Used exclusively for **past situations** that are now impossible to change. It represents zero possibility and is often used to express regret or relief.
* **Formulation:** `If + Past Perfect (had done)`, `would have + past participle (done)`
* **Examples:**
  * *If* I **had seen** her yesterday, I **would have spoken** to her.
  * I wouldn't have let them in *if* they **had been** late.

---

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Advanced Nuances: Reducing Certainty</span>

If you want to express that a consequence is possible but not 100% guaranteed, you can replace **will** or **would** with modal verbs of possibility (**may, might, could**).

* **In Conditional 1:** Use *may, might,* or *could*.
  * *If* I see her tomorrow, I **may/might/could speak** to her. (Doubt is introduced).
* **In Conditional 2:** Use *might* or *could*. (Never use *may* here).
  * *If* they were late again, I **might not let** them in.
* **In Conditional 3:** Use *might have* or *could have*.
  * *If* I had had some time last week, I **could have tidied** my office.

> **Warning:** Do not use **should** as a replacement for *will* or *would* in the consequence clause. (e.g., Say *"I would say hello"*, not *"I should say hello"*).

---

## <span style="color: #d4af37">Common Mistakes to Avoid</span>

**Mistake 1: Using "When" instead of "If" in real conditionals (Types 1, 2, 3)**
* Using "when" in Conditional 1 changes the meaning to absolute certainty.

❌ WRONG: When it were a little warmer, I would go for a swim.
✅ RIGHT: If it were a little warmer, I would go for a swim.

**Mistake 2: Using Will/Would twice (in both clauses)**
* The 'If Clause' must contain the standard tense, not the consequence modal (will/would).

❌ WRONG: If you will come back tomorrow...
✅ RIGHT: If you come back tomorrow...

❌ WRONG: You would miss the bus if you wouldn't leave now.
✅ RIGHT: You would miss the bus if you didn't leave now.

❌ WRONG: If you wouldn't have said anything...
✅ RIGHT: If you hadn't said anything...

**Mistake 3: Mixing Conditionals Incorrectly**
* Ensure your tenses consistently match the timeline and probability.

❌ WRONG: How much would it be if I pay cash?
✅ RIGHT (As Conditional 1): How much will it be if I pay cash?
✅ RIGHT (As Conditional 2): How much would it be if I paid cash?

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">IELTS Application</span>
> [!example] IELTS Example
> If stricter environmental laws were introduced, urban pollution would likely decline.

Use conditionals for:
- Task 2 solution and consequence arguments
- Speaking Part 3 hypothetical discussions
- Balanced reasoning with modals

## Common Errors
> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: If people will use buses, pollution will fall.
>
> Correct: If people use buses, pollution will fall.

> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: If I would have studied harder, I passed.
>
> Correct: If I had studied harder, I would have passed.
