# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Cleft Sentences</span>

## <span style="color: #00838f">Core Idea</span>
> [!info] Rule
> Cleft sentences split one idea into two parts to add focus and emphasis.

They help you highlight the most important cause, person, time, or solution.

## <span style="color: #00838f">It-Cleft</span>
Pattern:
- It is/was + focused part + that/who + clause

Examples:
- It is poor planning that causes severe traffic congestion.
- It was the teacher who encouraged me to improve my writing.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Wh-Cleft (Pseudo-Cleft)</span>
Pattern:
- What + clause + be + focused part

Examples:
- What cities need most is reliable public transport.
- What I learned from the experience was patience.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Reverse Wh-Cleft</span>
Pattern:
- Focused part + be + what + clause

Example:
- A long-term policy is what this sector requires.

## <span style="color: #00838f">All-Cleft</span>
Pattern:
- All + clause + be + focused part

Examples:
- All we need is a practical implementation plan.
- All the committee wanted was clearer evidence.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">IELTS Application</span>
> [!example] IELTS Example
> It is access to quality education that can reduce long-term inequality.

Use cleft sentences for:
- Task 2 thesis emphasis
- body paragraph topic focus
- strong conclusion framing

## <span style="color: #d4af37">Common Errors</span>
> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: It is governments should invest more.
>
> Correct: It is governments that should invest more.

> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: What is important that strict laws are enforced.
>
> Correct: What is important is that strict laws are enforced.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Actionable Application</span>
- Use one cleft sentence in introduction or conclusion for clear emphasis.
- Keep structure simple and grammatically complete.
- During editing, test whether the focused part is truly the key message.
