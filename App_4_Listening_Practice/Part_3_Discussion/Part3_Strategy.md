# Part 3: Discussion Strategy

## Part 3 Overview
- **Duration**: ~7-8 minutes (including reading and answering)
- **Questions**: 10
- **Format**: Conversation between 2-4 speakers (usually tutor/expert and student/colleague)
- **Topics**: Academic projects, assignments, research, problem-solving, professional scenarios

## Key Characteristics
- **More complex vocabulary** than Parts 1-2
- Multiple speakers with **different perspectives**
- Natural speech patterns with interruptions and repairs
- Ideas develop through discussion
- **Not scripted** - more spontaneous than Part 2
- May include disagreement or debate

## Listening Strategies

### Before Listening
1. **Study ALL 10 questions** thoroughly
2. **Identify speakers** (Interviewer A, Interviewee B, etc.) from question context
3. **Note question progression** - usually follows discussion order
4. **Predict complexity level** - will affect listening speed adjustment needed

### During Listening
1. **Distinguish between speakers** - who is speaking matters
2. **Follow the logic** of the discussion:
   - Who proposes an idea?
   - Who agrees/disagrees?
   - What modifications are made?
3. **Listen for agreement/disagreement markers**:
   - "I agree, but..."
   - "That's a good point..."
   - "I'm not sure about that..."
   - "Actually..."
4. **Note when ideas change** - earlier statements may be contradicted
5. **Track solutions and conclusions** - often at the end

### After Listening
1. **Verify which speaker gave the answer**
2. **Check that answer reflects discussion accurately**
3. **Review any missed or unclear sections**

## Common Question Types

### Multiple Choice
- More complex options
- Distractors include mentioned-but-rejected ideas
- Requires understanding entire discussion context

### Short Answer
- Requires specific information from discussion
- Must match what was actually said/agreed upon

### Matching
- Match speakers/opinions to statements
- Requires careful identification of who said what

### True/False/Not Given
- Requires deep comprehension
- "Not Given" is common - distinguishes high scorers

## Common Traps
- ❌ Attributing idea to wrong speaker
- ❌ Choosing rejected ideas (mentioned but not agreed upon)
- ❌ Missing nuance in conditional statements
- ❌ Confusing "they discussed" with "they concluded"
- ❌ Not noting changes in opinion mid-discussion
- ❌ Assuming speaker agreement when they're just being polite

## Vocabulary Focus
- Academic and professional terminology
- Discourse markers and connectors
- Hedging language ("sort of," "kind of," "I suppose")
- Modal verbs (possibility, necessity, advice)
- Complex linking phrases

## Speaker Identification Tips
- Pay attention to **name use** - speakers often use each other's names
- Note **role markers** - "as you know," "the thing is," different tones
- Track **topic shifts** - who introduces new ideas?
- Listen for **disagreement signals** - they often clarify who's saying what
