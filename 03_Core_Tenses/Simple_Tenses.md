# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Simple Tenses</span>

Simple tenses express facts, habits, completed actions, and straightforward future plans. They're the most direct way to communicate. In IELTS, simple tenses form the core of your sentences—often paired with more complex tenses for variety and sophistication.

---

## <span style="color: #00838f">Present Simple</span>

Use present simple for facts that are always true, habits that repeat, and scheduled events.

**Form:**  
Affirmative: Subject + base verb (+ s/es) + object  
Negative: Subject + do/does not + base verb + object  
Question: Do/Does + subject + base verb + object?  
Negative question: Do/Does + subject + not + base verb + object?

**When to use in IELTS:**

In Task 2, you use present simple for thesis statements and universal truths:  
"Education increases social mobility." (General fact that's always true)

In Task 1, present simple describes what the graph shows:  
"The chart illustrates consumption trends from 2010 to 2020." (What the visual displays)

In Speaking, it describes routines and habits:  
"I study in the evening because the house is quieter." (What you regularly do)

Examples:
- Although she works full-time, she writes a letter to her grandmother every Sunday.
- She writes a letter when she has free time, and she adds photos so her grandmother can see her progress.
- She does not write letters on weekdays because she returns home late, but she usually writes on Saturday mornings.
- Do you write to your relatives when you travel, or do you prefer sending quick messages?
- Does she write a draft first so she can avoid mistakes?

## <span style="color: #00838f">Simple Past</span>
Usage:
- completed actions in the past
- past habits

Formulas:
- Affirmative: Subject + V2 + object
- Negative: Subject + did not + base verb + object
- Question: Did + subject + base verb + object?
- Negative question: Did + subject + not + base verb + object?

Examples:
- She wrote a letter after she finished her shift, and she posted it before the rain started.
- Because the internet failed that evening, she wrote a long letter instead of sending an email.
- She did not write to him when she was in Dhaka, but she called him every night.
- Did she write the report before the manager arrived, or did she finish it later?
- Did she write to you after she heard the results?

## <span style="color: #00838f">Simple Future</span>
Usage:
- decisions, predictions, and future actions

Formulas:
- Affirmative: Subject + will/shall + base verb + object
- Negative: Subject + will/shall not + base verb + object
- Question: Will/Shall + subject + base verb + object?
- Negative question: Will/Shall + subject + not + base verb + object?

Examples:
- She will write a letter when she reaches London, and she will include the travel details.
- If the office remains closed tomorrow, she will write from home and send the document by noon.
- She will not write the final version until the supervisor checks the outline, but she will prepare notes tonight.
- Will she write to the client after the meeting ends, or will she call first?
- Will she write the summary if the data arrives on time?

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">IELTS Application</span>
> [!example] IELTS Example
> Writing Task 1: The number of commuters rose sharply in 2015, and it remained high until the end of the decade.

> [!example] IELTS Example
> Writing Task 2: Public transport reduces congestion because it carries large numbers of passengers, and it lowers fuel consumption in cities.

> [!example] IELTS Example
> Speaking: I usually study in the evening because the house is quieter, and I can focus better then.

## <span style="color: #d4af37">Common Errors</span>
> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: She write a letter every day.
>
> Correct: She writes a letter every day.

> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: Did she wrote a letter?
>
> Correct: Did she write a letter?

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Functional Use (Advanced)</span>
- Use simple present for thesis statements, definitions, and stable facts in Writing Task 2.
- Use simple past for completed events, dated trends, and narrative steps in Speaking Part 2.
- Use simple future for decisions, promises, and prediction language in conclusions.
- Use present simple (not future) in time clauses after when, before, after, until, as soon as, and if.
- Use simple forms to keep sentences clear, then expand with complex clauses for higher band range.

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">Advanced Tense Flow</span>
> [!tip] Flow Pattern
> Present claim -> Past evidence -> Future recommendation

- Public transport reduces congestion, and the 2019 data confirmed this trend, so governments will invest more in metro systems.
- If cities improve bus coverage, more people will leave private cars, and traffic will decline.
- When the chart presents a finished year, describe it in simple past even if the report is written now.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Grammar Rules Checklist</span>
- Subject-verb agreement is mandatory in simple present: he/she/it + V1+s/es.
- Use do/does/did for negatives and questions; the main verb stays in base form.
- Do not use two tense markers in one verb group: did wrote is incorrect.
- Use simple present for universal truths and scheduled timetables.
- Use simple past with finished past time markers: yesterday, last year, in 2018.
- Use simple future with will for instant decisions and predictions.
- In first conditional, use present simple in the if-clause and will + base verb in the result clause.
- After modal verbs, always use base verb.
- Keep time reference consistent inside each sentence unless a contrast is intended.
- Use adverbs of frequency before main verbs but after be: she usually studies, she is usually punctual.
