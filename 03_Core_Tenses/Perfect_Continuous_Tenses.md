# <span style="color: #1a3a52">Perfect Continuous Tenses</span>

## <span style="color: #00838f">Core Idea</span>
> [!info] Rule
> Perfect Continuous tenses emphasize duration up to a reference point.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Present Perfect Continuous</span>
Usage:
- action started in the past and still continuing
- emphasis on duration and ongoing activity

Formulas:
- Affirmative: Subject + has/have + been + base verb + ing + object
- Negative: Subject + has/have not + been + base verb + ing + object
- Question: Has/Have + subject + been + base verb + ing + object?
- Negative question: Has/Have + subject + not + been + base verb + ing + object?

Examples:
- She has been writing in English every evening, and she has been tracking her mistakes in a notebook.
- Because she has been practicing complex sentences for months, she has been expressing her ideas more clearly.
- She has not been writing on social media lately because she has been focusing on IELTS tasks, but she has been reading model answers.
- Has she been writing with a timer since the coaching classes began, or has she been practicing without time limits?
- Has she been writing longer introductions because she has been following the new template?

## <span style="color: #00838f">Past Perfect Continuous</span>
Usage:
- action continuing up to another past point

Formulas:
- Affirmative: Subject + had + been + base verb + ing + object
- Negative: Subject + had not + been + base verb + ing + object
- Question: Had + subject + been + base verb + ing + object?
- Negative question: Had + subject + not + been + base verb + ing + object?

Examples:
- She had been writing for two hours before the teacher arrived, and she had been revising each paragraph carefully.
- Because she had been preparing all week, she had been writing more confidently when the mock test started.
- She had not been writing consistently before January because she had been working overtime, but she had been studying on weekends.
- Had she been writing in pencil because her pen had been leaking, or was that her usual habit?
- Had she been writing to multiple institutions before she finally received an offer?

## <span style="color: #00838f">Future Perfect Continuous</span>
Usage:
- action continuing up to a specific future time

Formulas:
- Affirmative: Subject + will/shall + have been + base verb + ing + object
- Negative: Subject + will/shall not + have been + base verb + ing + object
- Question: Will/Shall + subject + have been + base verb + ing + object?
- Negative question: Will/Shall + subject + not + have been + base verb + ing + object?

Examples:
- By July, she will have been writing IELTS essays for a full year, and she will have been attending weekly feedback sessions.
- When the exam date arrives, she will have been writing under timed conditions for months because her mentor insisted on realism.
- She will not have been writing daily by then if her internship schedule remains this heavy, but she will have been practicing whenever she can.
- Will she have been writing with advanced connectors for long enough to use them naturally in the test?
- By the end of this term, will she have been writing both Task 1 and Task 2 every week so that her stamina improves?

## <span style="color: #5e35b1">IELTS Application</span>
> [!example] IELTS Example
> Speaking Part 1: I have been preparing for IELTS for six months, so my confidence has been growing steadily.

> [!example] IELTS Example
> Speaking Part 2: I had been waiting for the bus for 40 minutes before it came, and by the time it arrived, I was exhausted.

> [!example] IELTS Example
> Task 2 context: Urban populations have been growing rapidly over the last decade, and governments have been struggling to expand infrastructure at the same pace.

## <span style="color: #d4af37">Common Errors</span>
> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: I have been study English.
>
> Correct: I have been studying English.

> [!warning] Band Penalty
> Incorrect: She had been worked for two years.
>
> Correct: She had been working for two years.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Functional Use (Advanced)</span>
- Use present perfect continuous to stress ongoing effort and duration up to now.
- Use past perfect continuous to show long-running background activity before a past event.
- Use future perfect continuous to project sustained activity up to a future point.
- Prefer perfect continuous when process and effort matter more than a finished result.
- Use these tenses to show persistence, training cycles, and trend pressure in IELTS arguments.

## <span style="color: #1b5e20">Advanced Tense Flow</span>
> [!tip] Flow Pattern
> Ongoing duration (perfect continuous) -> Outcome (perfect or simple)

- Urban populations have been rising for years, so housing costs have increased in almost every major city.
- She had been practicing under timed conditions for months before she took the exam, and she performed calmly.
- By June, I will have been preparing for one year, and I will have completed at least fifty full essays.

## <span style="color: #00838f">Grammar Rules Checklist</span>
- Present perfect continuous: has/have + been + V-ing.
- Past perfect continuous: had + been + V-ing.
- Future perfect continuous: will have been + V-ing.
- Use for to show duration and since to show starting points.
- Use dynamic verbs in V-ing forms; avoid stative verbs in most formal contexts.
- Use perfect continuous for incomplete, ongoing activity linked to a reference point.
- Use perfect (not perfect continuous) when completion result is the focus.
- In negatives, place not after the first auxiliary.
- In questions, invert the first auxiliary with the subject.
- Keep timeline logic explicit with markers such as for three years, since 2020, by next July.
